Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

 DNA methylation is one of the most important functional alterations that accurse in epigenetics. In general where DNA methylation is found transcription factors of the cell cannot bind due to the methyl groups that block this connection.  Therefor it is vital for this phenomenon to take place in  specific chromosomal departments meaning that any differention from normal may lead to a tone of epigenetic problems such as cancer. 

DNA methylation is typically found on and closely around the CpG island clusters some hundreds base pairs upstream of almost every gene. Its normal function is to control gene expression. Essentially when CpG islands are extensively methylated this usually leads to the genes under expression or even the specific genes silencing. 
Cancer is situation where a lot of genetic and epigenetic abnormalities are present. Overall CpG islands tend to be over methylated in cancer leading to silencing of genes, especially those genes that lead to cell death signaling however in the case of oncogenes (genes that promote cell growth) CpG islands are demethylated leading to cell proliferation and high cell division rate. In this manner we can assume that in cancer most oncogenes are upregulated contributing to the cells indefinite survival in contrast to genes that control the cells apoptosis path where CpG islands methylation is abundantly present.


As for the repetitive elements and the intergenic regions these are positions that are normally found methylated in the genome. This is logical for these regions are found throughout the genes and must be methylated to not allow elements to bind in the middle of genes disrupting its transcriptional control. In an opposite manner of CpG islands, these regions are methylated in the normal genome but unmethylated in cancer so as to disrupt the cells stability by allowing recombination’s and silencing where not needed.

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.
ICR methylation is another important aspect in epigenetics. In the case of imprinted genes there is a pattern that determines which allele will be expressed for the specific gene. (The paternal or maternal) In the case of the H19/Igf2 cluster it is predetermined by methylation patterns on the ICR region of the paternal allele that the H19 gene must be transcribed by the maternally derived chromosome in contrast to the Igf2 gene that must be transcribed by the paternal allele solely. This happens because when the ICR region is methylated CTCF complex cannot be recruited leading to the silencing of the H19 gene but expression of Igf2. When this pattern is disrupted by methylation on both ICRs we have 100% silencing of the H19 gene and double expression of the Igf2 gene since both patterns are now similar and paternal like. This leads to over expression of Igf2 a growth promoting gene that will eventually turn the normal cell to a cancer cell. This is a commonly found as an early event and is described as Wilm’s tumor.

Altering DNA methylation patterns may lead to differentiation of the chromosomal architecture, recombination and also silencing of vital to the cells survival genes. Epigenetic changes that happen during chemotherapy with a drug that alters the epigenetic state are passed on during cell division to daughter and granddaughter cells until they are actively erased. Once erased, though, they do not return. This is how these drugs tend to survive beyond the period of treatment. When assuming an epigenetic drug it is vital to be very careful of the exact timing of the treatment so as to avoid any sensitive epigenetic periods. The most important period of life which has the biggest impact on the epigenome is that of primordial gem cell development. Therefor it is highly inadvisable to treat a pregnant woman with such a drug in this period for not only will the effect of the drug affect the patient but it will also have devastating effects on her carrying child and her grandchildren for  during primordial germ cell development we can find the highest rate of epigenetic reprograming. In addition the preimplantation period is also a sensitive state when epigenetic drug treatment is inadvisable.
