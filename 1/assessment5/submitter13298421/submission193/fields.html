Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 
Decitabine or 5-alza-2'-desoxycytidine is a cytidine analog, which works as an specific inhibitor of DNA methylation and it is used as a treatment of myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS) but It also can be used to the treatment of solid tumors (1,2,3).

The hypermethylation of some promoter genes (CpG) islands in cancer is correlated with a low gene expression. Decitabine acts as a potent inhibitor of the DNA methyltransferases (DNMT) induced an hypomethylation of DNA in neoplastic cells (4, 5). Decitabine get incorporated into the DNA blocking DNMT action but this function is devision dependent, need cell replication to work (6).

Unlike genetic lesions, epigenetic alteration can be pharmacologically reversible in these cases the hypomethylation induced by Decitabine can allow to the reactivation of silent tumor-suppressor genes, cell differentiation, growth suppressor and loss of pluripotency and it was linked with anti-tumour effects. (2, 4, 5, 6).

Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

DNA methylation is the addition of methyl groups to the DNA and it occurs almost exclusively at Cs of CpG dinucleotide. DNA methylation is laid down by methyltramsferases (DNMT3a and DMNT3b) and maintained through the development by DMNT1. Most of CpG clusters are into the CpG islands often at the promoters of genes and it tends to be protected from methylation. CpG methylated at the CpG island is associated with the formation of a repressive chromatine structure and  it means the silencing of the gene expression, X inactivation etc (1, 2).

In spite of CpG islands are protected from DNA methylation in cancer cells promoter it tends to be hypermetylated. This hypermetylated of CpG islands in cancer cells are correlated with silencing of  tumor-suppressor genes, which controlling cell cycle, apoptosis or DNA repair. It contributed to abnormal development of cells at last it leads to tumourigenesis (2).

Intergenic regions and repetitive elements usually are highly methylated and the function of this methylation is maintained genome integrity in fact the cells without DMNT1 display genome instability.  (1, 2). In normal cells Intergenic regions and repetitive elements are silenced by DNA methylation to prevent aberrations due to genome instability (2, 4) DNA hypomethylation was the initial epigenetic abnormality found in human tumors and it occurred early in tumourigenesis. Hypomethylation can leads to  delections, reciprocal translocation  and indirect effects on gene expression (3)

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.
Igf2 or insulin - like growth factor 2 is a gene which have a parental expression regulated by DNA methylation. In normal cells paternal allele is expressed and maternal allele is silenced by methylation of the CpG island on the gene promoters (1,2).  However in some cancers both alleles, paternal and maternal, are expressed. It's a consequence of the loss of the imprinting in the maternal allele (3).

Igf2 and H19 genes are reciprocally imprinted, expression of H19 is associated with silencing of IGF2 and vice versa, that is when the paternal allele of Igf2 is expressed H19 is silenced, in the maternal allele occurs the opposite (4). Loss of imprinting of Igf2 and silencing of H19 promoter by methylation can result in uncontrolled cell growth and division that are two of cancer features. 

In Wilms' tumors the expression of H19 and Igf2 is deregulated, in this cases we can observe the expression of both Igf2 alleles, maternal and paternal, and the silenced of both H19 alleles. It's correlated with a prolliferative advantage and overgrown (5,6).
