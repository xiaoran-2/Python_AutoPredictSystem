Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.
P { margin-bottom: 0.08in; }

A pattern of DNA methylation will persist through somatic cell replication throughout the life of an individual. Such patterns will be erased during embryonic development and during the development of primordial germ cells for the reproduction of the individual. These are called sensitive periods during which the cell development process is extremely sensitive to environmental disturbance, which could adversely effect the development of the individual or his/her offspring.



Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 
P { margin-bottom: 0.08in; }

Decitabine is a DNA-demethylating agent. It acts by decreasing the methylation of parts of the DNA molecules (human chromosomes). It is most effective in treating cancer by decreasing hypermethylation at CpG islands, which is a concomitant of some cancers. Such treatment must be careful to minimize demethylation of other areas of the genome where decreased methylation has the opposite effect (such as intergenic and repeat segments).



Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.
P { margin-bottom: 0.08in; }

On the paternal allele hypermethylation occurs on H19 and ICR so the activators can reach Igf2, the expression of which is, thereby, enhanced. On the maternal allele there is little methylation on H19 or ICR, so CTCF binds on the ICR and the activators cannot reach Igf2, which, therefore, remains unexpressed. In the case of Wilm’s tumor hypermethylation occurs on the maternal ICR (as well as on the paternal allele). This prevents CTCF from binding and leaves the activators to promote the expression of Igf2 just as on the paternal allele. There is now a double dose of Igf2, which is a strongly growth producing agent . This double dose (of both maternal and paternal origin), can enhance Wilm's tumor growth, as well as other forms of cancer. In fact the hypermethylation of the ICR may be a precursor for many other forms of cancer.




Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

P { margin-bottom: 0.08in; }

In normal cells the CpG islands are not highly methylated. In many cancerous cells the CpG islands become hypermethylated; this hypermethylation may suppress many genes that play a role in the suppression of uncontrolled growth. The intergenic regions and repetitive elements of normal chromosomes are strongly methylated. Such methylation is strongly decreased in cancerous cells. Growth promoting genes, which would normally be suppressed by normal methylation of these regions, will now be released to enhance the spread of cancer.
