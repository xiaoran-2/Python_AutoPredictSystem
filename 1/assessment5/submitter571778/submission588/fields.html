Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

Methylation describes the process of adding a methyl group on a cytosine, which in mammals is followed by a Guanine; these regions are the so-called CpG islands. When localized in a promotor gene, methylation is normally related to gene expression. Nevertheless, when promoter regions are hypermethylated, the result is normally the silencing of the gene. In cancer cells, not only does hypermethylation occurs, but it has also a different pattern than in normal cells; in cancer, it's possible to observe a hypomethylation of other areas except for CpG islands, such as intergenic regions.

How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable. 
A sensitive period is a moment during development in which the organism's epigenetic marks are reset. There are two major sensitive periods during mammals' development: the primordial germ cell development period and the "pre and early post-implantation"period. As we do inherit gene expression patterns from our parents, modifications, especially those occurring during gamete development, can be passed forward to the offspring.

Decitabine is a DNA-demethylating agent, that works as an inhibitor to DNA methyltransferase. That results in hypomethylation of hypermethylated regions, reverting gene silencing and allowing normal expression of  p15INK4B. When this happens, the cancer cell starts to express suppressor genes and to differentiate. Those activities combined result in tumor suppression.

Imprinted genes commonly express only one of the alleles. The Insulin-like growth factor 2 (IGF2) is imprinted in a way that only the paternal allele is expressed. The Loss of imprinting activates the gene that is normally inactivated, in this case, the maternal allele. When this happens, both of the genes are silenced.  The loss of imprinting in IGF2 is the most common factor behind Willms tumors, and it was in this kind of tumors that it was first observed. The activation of IGF2 seems to have a positive effect over tumor growth since it appears to reduce apoptosis.
