Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

 In a normal cell, Igf is found to be paternally imprinted e.g. the paternal allele is found to be methylated in the H19/Igf2 cluster, while the maternal allele is unmethylated. The unmethylated allele is bonded to the CTCF complex, enhancing H19 e.g. CTC´s binding insulate Igf, letting the enhancers free to act on H19. In the paternal allele, CTCF is not bonded to the paternal strand to inhibit the enhancers to act on Igf2 and therefore , Igf2 is enhanced and H19 is silenced. To summarize, a normal cell inherit a normal dose of H19 and Igf2 from the maternal and paternal strand, respectively. However, disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to Wilm´s tumour, once both allele will behave as paternal allele e.g. maternal allele will also be hypermthylated and, therefore, double dose of Igf2  enhancement and H19 silencing will be observed.

DNA methylation is found to influence gene transcription/silencing beyond the period of drug treatment, once this epigenetic alteration is highly preserved during mitosis and can be trans-generationally inherited through the gametes. This is only possible because epigenetic modification is susceptible to environment change, or in this context, chemical therapy.
