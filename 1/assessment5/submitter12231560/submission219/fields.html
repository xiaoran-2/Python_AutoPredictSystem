Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

CpG islands are dinucleotides that found in clusters in the promoter regions upstream at the 5' end of Tc start site and spread throughout the genome. They are unmethylated in a normal state at the promoter regions and methylated globally in other regions such as intergenic and repetitive elements. In a diseased state like cancer, an opposite phenomenon is observed. The CpGs are hypermethylated resulting in silencing of genes. So when a tumor suppressor gene like Retinoblastoma (Rb) is hypermethylated, the gene is transcriptionally silenced causing tumorgenesis. When a gene is hypermethylated, the methyl binding proteins (meCp2) are recruited to these regions that HDAC bind to causing chromatin wound up to heterochromatin and gene silencing.These aberrant changes are then inherited and passed on to daughter cells. CpGs are hypomethylated globally (at a protooncogene). This results in chromatin rearrangement flaws, recomination, deletion flaws. There also is loss of imprinting of genes (Igf2) that leads to colorectal cancer.
