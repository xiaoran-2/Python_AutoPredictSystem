Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

DNA methylation refers to the addition of a methyl (CH3) group to the cytosine or guanine nucleotides. This methyl group may be added to the fifth carbon atom of the "Cytosine" base or the sixth nitrogen atom of the "Guanine"  base.

This modification of the DNA alters the genes expressed in cells when they divide and differentiate from embryonic stem cells into cells of a particular tissue.

A CpG island are clusters of dinucleotides often found at promoters of genes. Al though CpG islands have a high occurrence of Cytosine and Guanine, CpG islands tend to be protected from DNA methylation. DNA methylation of CpG islands will result in silencing of the gene expression.

Intergenic regions and repetitive elements  of a gene are usually methylated. This is done in order to maintain genomic stability. So that during transcription of the gene there will be less mistakes (transpositions, transcriptional interference of illegitimate recombination’s) because mistakes will lead to genomic instability.

In cancer often the CpG  islands undergo hypermethylation, adding DNA methylation to the CpG islands. Because DNA methylation of CpG islangs results in silencing of gene expression, this will make a cell function different than before. The DNA methylation can be passed on to daughter cells during cell division, which will give more cells that function different than before.

In cancer often the Intergenic regions and repetitive elements undergo hypomethylation, removing DNA methylation of the Intergenic regions and repetitive elements. The Intergenic regions and repetitive elements  are methylated to keep genomic stability, if the methylation is removed this will make the cells genomic instable. Because during transcription there is a lot more gene to transcribe the when Intergenic regions and repetitive elements  are methylated which will increase the change of mistakes.

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

Around the H19/Igf2 locus ordinarily there is methylation of the paternal allele at the ICR, and no methylation for the maternal allele.

The CTCF complex binds to the allele on the maternal strand and the downstream enhancers act on H19 as a result.

The CTCF complex does not bind to the paternal allele due to the methyl marks, which leads to silencing of the H19 gene as the enhancers instead drive expression of Igf2.

In a regular cell there will be a normal dose of both H19 from the maternal allele and Igf2 from the paternal allele.

When ICR becomes hypermethylated, as in Wilm's tumour,  a cell will have two dose of Igf2, which is growth promoting, from maternal and paternal allele and no dose of H19.
