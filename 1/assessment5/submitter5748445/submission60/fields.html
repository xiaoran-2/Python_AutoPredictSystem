Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

  

Normally DNA methylation at CpG islands (at the 5′ position of the cytosine ring within CpG dinucleotides) do the following functions:

Represses the oncogenes expression at these specific sites to prevent uncontrolled cell division. On the contrary the prevention of methylation of tumor suppressor genes activates the normal suppression functions of these genes and prevent tumorigenesis.Additionally DNA methylation helps in controlling cell cycle according to the cellular needs i.e stop the cycle and allowing DNA repair or in other situations promote apoptosis. Also, CpG islands are methylated on X chromosome and inactivate it in female mammals.

The disturbance of the DNA CpG islands methylation homeostasis may promote carcinogenesis as follows:

Hypermethylation of DNA CpG islands (which are usually unmethylated) can silence the tumor suppressor genes, Can cause loss of control on cell cycle, leads to disturbance or loss of apoptosis, and may cause loss of DNA repair mechanisms.

For example: In colorectal cancer, there is a high frequency of DNA methylation at CpG islands. Another example is the de novo methyl transferase, DNMT3a (one of the enzymes which is responsible for DNA methylation) mutation in acute myeloid leukemia (AML).

The intergenic regions and repetitive elements promote chromosomal stability and genomic integrity and they are usually methylated. The hypomethylation of them can lead to chromosomal instability and increase translocations, insertions, or deletions. These chromosomal abnormalities may lead to carcinogenesis for example if a part of a chromosome containing oncogene is translocated into normal gene or into a tumor suppressor gene, the normal function of these genes will be lost and the cell continue to divide uncontrollably.








Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.
 

In normal cell the area around H19/Igf2 locus is methylated in the paternal allele at the ICR (Imprint Control Regions) but the maternal allele is not methylated. So that, in the maternal strand the CTCF complex binds to the allele and the enhancers act on H19 and activate it. However, the methylation on the paternal allele prevents the CTCF complex from binding of enhancers on the H19 and instead, activates Igf2 expression i.e the methyl marks act as insulator from H19 binding. Thus in a normal cell, the maternal strand show active H19 and the paternal strand shows active Igf2 instead. However, if ICR becomes hypermethylated, both strands look like the paternal part and the Igf2 growth factor is activated as twice as normal without any suppressor gene as in Wilm’s tumor. So that the double activation of a growth factor gene expression and inhibition of a tumor suppressor gene may leads to uncontrolled cell division and carcinogenesis.





Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 
 

Decitabine is a cytidine analogue acts as demethylating agent on DNA. The process of DNA hypermethylation can block the activity of the tumor suppressor genes and promotes carcinogenesis as in myelodysplastic syndrome. So that, by demethylating agents as decitabine the tumor suppressor genes are reactivated and act to stop the tumor progression and can be used to treat myelodysplastic syndromes, the precursors of acute myelogenous leukemia.





Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.
 

DNA methylation is a part of epigenetic processes and it is mitotically heritable but it is reversible so that it is an attractive target for treatments. Additionally once modified, it cannot be returned. However these types of interfering treatments may alter the normal biochemical functions of the cell especially during the critical or sensitive periods which are preimplantation period (or even before fertilization) and during gametogenesis in males and females and this occurs mainly by reprograming the process of methylation after clearance, so the paternal part undergoes active demethylation and the maternal part undergoes passive demethylation (by dilution effect) . Hence hypomethylation or hypermethylathion by drugs during these periods can lead to loss or gain of unsuitable gene function. So that it is not advisable to use these drugs during these sensitive periods or epigenetic reprograming periods.
