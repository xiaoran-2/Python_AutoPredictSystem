Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

DNA methylation consists of the addition of a methyl group to the 5 position of cytosine. This chemical change switches off gene expression. CpG islands (CGI) are clusters of the dinucleotide CG, localized throughout the genome, just upstream the promoter of many genes. Around 60 % of the promoters have CGI. Although methylation only occurs in CpG, CGI are usually unmethylated, so the promoter is active and the nearby gene is normally expressed. Nonetheless, in many tumours CGI become heavily methylated, silencing the expression of the underlying gene. Many genes coding for proteins that inhibit cell growth became inactivated, giving rise to uncontrolled cell division. Hypermethylation not only involves CGI, but also the neighbour regions called CGI shores. CGI and CGI shore hypermethylation is one of the disorders that may contribute to the development of cancer.

Methylation elsewhere in the genome shows a different distribution. Unlike CGI, intergenic regions are usually methylated in normal cells to avoid aberrant reading frames in a gene (e.g. an antisense 3'-5' reading frame within an intron). Repetitive elements are parasitic DNA sequences that tend to make copies of themselves and insert anywhere in the genome. Methylation of these repetitive elements aids to keep genome integrity by stopping transposition of these elements, that may result in gene disruption, transcriptional interference, activation of repressed promoters or abnormal recombination. In cancer, over 50 % of physiological methylation is lost. Hypomethylation particularly affects intergenic regions and repetitive elements, resulting in genome instability. Genes that control cell division may become disrupted Other ones that promote cell growth may be disinhibit by activation of normally repressed or even newly created promoters. The alteration in cell division by both these mechanisms contribute to development of cancer.
Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.
Imprinting is an epigenetic mechanism by which only one parental allele is expressed. The other one is inactivated through methylation. An imprinting control region (ICR) is necessary for imprinting to be carried out. 

H19/Igf2 cluster (11q15.5) comprises Igf2, an ICR, H19 and genes coding for enhacers that act  both on H19 and Igf2. In the paternal allele, ICR is methylated and methylation spreads through the promoter of H19, silencing its expression. The enhancers encoded downstream promote freely Igf2 expression and therefore cell growth. So H19 is paternally imprinted. In the maternal allele, ICR is not methylated and binds a protein called CTCF. H19 is transcribed normally. CTCF stops the dowstream enhancers from reaching Igf2, so this gene is silenced. The enhacers act exclusively on H19 expression. H19 encodes an RNA that limits cell growth. So, Igf2 is maternally imprinted.

In Wilm's tumour there is loss of Igf2 imprinting by hypermethylation of the maternal allele (ICR and H19 promoter), so that CTCF does not bind ICR and H19 is not expressed. Thus both alleles promote Igf2 expression and repress H19. This change results in excessive cell growth (from twice the normal amount of Igf2 and no H19 expression) and cancer development.
Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 
Decitabine (5-aza-2'-deoxycytidine) is an analogue of desoxiribocytosine. It decreases DNA methylation by inhibiting DNA methyltransferases (DNMTs), both DNMT1 and DNMT3. Decitabine is incorpotated to the newly formed DNA strand during replication as if it were desoxiribocytosine. DNMT recognizes decitabine as a substrate and binds it but, unable to get free, establishes an irreversible covalent bond. This union between drug and enzyme is cut by the repairing DNA enzymes. Thus the activity of DNMT and therefore DNA methylation are notably reduced.

In some haematological neoplasias, such as myelodisplasic syndromes, gene promoters that control cell growth are hypermethylated, giving rise to uncontrolled cell division. Decitabine has proved useful to treat this sort of tumours by inhibiting methylation, allowing these tumour suppresor genes to be expressed.
Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.
Epigenetic marks, particularly DNA methylation, may have long lasting effects on the epigenome of the cells involved. Intake of methyl donors, such as folate, by the mother may change the DNA methylation pattern in the offpring, at least in mice, and give rise to long lasting consequences such as skin colour or problems with sugar metabolism. DNA methylation pattern is inherited with each mitosis. X chromosome inactivation is based on DNA methylation and occurs at random in cells during early development states. The cells derived from a given mother cell maintain the same inactivated X chromosome no matter how many divisions occur throughout the life of the individual.

Depending on the moment of development, the epigenome may be more or less susceptible to external stimuli. In periods when epigenetic marks are erased to be reprogrammed, the cell is particularly sensitive to enviromental stimuli. These moments of development are called sensitive periods and occur during preimplantation development (around 10 days after fertilization) and during germ cell development. It is not a good idea to treat patients (or pregnant mothers) during sensitive periods as drugs may interfere with epigenome laying down and result in serious conditions such as Angelman syndrome or  tumours.
