Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

DNA methylation is essential  for normal development in mammals. In a normal healthy cell CpG islands are hypomethylated in contrast with the intergenic regions, the repetitive elements and introns which are hypermethylated.  Cytosines in CpG dinucleotides can be methylated by DNA methyltransferases (DNMTs) enzymes such as Dnmt3a and Dnmt3b to form 5-methylcytosine . Dnmt3a and Dnmt3b are able to establish a new methylation pattern to the unmethylated DNA (de novo). Unlike Dnmt3a and Dnmt3b, Dnmt1 which maintains DNA methylation, functions during DNA replication to copy the DNA methylation pattern from the parental DNA strand onto the newly synthesized daughter strand. CpG islands tend to be protected from methylation and methylation of these CpG islands results in silencing of gene expression. In cancer, the intergenic regions, repetitive elements and introns are hypomethylated and this leads to copying errors including deletions, insertions and reciprocal translocations.In contrast, the CpG islands acquire abnormal hypermethylation which results in transcriptional silencing thus gene inactivation; such as silencing of tumor suppressor genes in cancer cells that is inherited in daughter cells following division which give rise to more tumor cells.

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.
Expression of imprinted genes is controlled by imprint control regions (ICRs). Imprint is associated with DNA methylation at ICR. The ICR at Igf2 and H19 is normally methylated on the paternal allele. Methylation is excluded from the maternal allele by CTCF binding.
In the maternal strand, the CTCF complex binds to the allele due to methylation marks which then lead to silencing of H19 as the enhancers initiate the expression of Igf2. In a normal cell there is a normal dose of H19 from the maternal strand and Igf2 from the paternal strand.Sometimes however,there can be hypermethylation at the ICR which is associated with Wilm's tumour. In such cases,the two strands will be inherited from the father and will be expressed as the paternal which means that the gene will have a double dose of Igf2 and zero dose of H19.
Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 
Decitabine is a DNA methyltransferase inhibitor. It inhibits DNA methylation so DNA becomes hypomethylated. Decitabine's activity is useful in treating cancers such as myelodysplatic syndrome  (Leukemia) because by the induction of hypomethylation, it is able to reactivate epigenetically silenced tumour suppressor genes.
Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.
DNA methylation is an excellent target for treatment of cancer.The reason of why DNA methylation is an excellent candidate for treatment of cancer is that it can be easily manipulated and enduring effects are ensured because DNA methyltransferase enzymes known as Dnmts preserve methyl marks during mitosis in cell division. A sensitive period is a period of time where extensive epigenetic reprogramming occurs. Treating patients during sensitive periods is inadvisable because some epigenetic drugs may interfere with the normal function of DNA methylation. Such an example is of pregnant women. Pregnant women are advised not to take epigenetic drugs not only during pregnancy but even months before conception because most reprogramming occurs in early child development as well as during the formation of gametes (oocytes in female and spermatocytes in male) and taking these epigenetic drugs could result in disease or even cancer in the embryo.
