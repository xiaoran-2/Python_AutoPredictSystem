Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

CpG islands are usually not methylated in normal genome, but in cancer there is a gain of methylation at these sites. For these regions are sometimes close to tumor supressor genes promoters, CpG island methylation inhibits their expression contributing to cell transformation. CpG islands next to promoters of oncogenes, instead, are usually demethylated.
Normally, repetitive elements and intergenic regions are methylated. This inhibits their transcription through cryptic promoters and through promoters of transposable elements. In cancer, these regions are hypomethylated which leads to genomic instability through transcription of transposable elements and cromosomal rearrangements, These events can trigger aberrant patterns of gene expression contributing to cancer.
Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.
In the paternal allele, a region upstream the Igf2 gene is not methylated so it is a viable site for binding of an insulator protein which inhibits transcription of Igf2 (localized downstream of this region) and enable the enhancers that are downstream of Igf2 to act on H19 thus promoting it's transcription.
In the maternal allele, the region upstream the Igf2 gene is methylated, so the insulator protein can't bind to the DNA. In this case, the enhancers that are downstream Igf2 aren't free to act on H19 and can act only in Igf2 causing it's transcription.
In a disease called Wilm's tumour, the affected individual may have one good paternal allele and one mutated maternal allele that behaves like a paternal one. So he ends with a duplicated product of H19 and no Igf2.
As H19 and Igf2 are involved in cell growth, impairment of this cluster have consequences on cell proliferation that may cause cancer.

Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.
During the process of cell division, one originally all-methylated locus on a double-strand of DNA becomes hemimethylated because of DNA duplication, but DNMT1 (which is an enzyme with affinity for hemimethylated DNA) can restore the methylated state on the non-methylated strand. Therefore, DNA methylation is an inheritable process and so are its effects.
There are periods of human development in which the DNA methylation patterns are reprogrammed and therefore are periods subject to errors or other interferences in this patterns. For this reason they are called sensitive periods. The two more important sensitive periods known are the early embryo development and the primordial germ cell (PGC) development.
Treatment of patients during such periods may cause effects that will be inherited by all the individual's cells or by the PGC's that will give birth to the individual's descendants. As some of these effects are undesirable for other periods of time that are not now, treatment in this condition turns not to be a good thing to do.
