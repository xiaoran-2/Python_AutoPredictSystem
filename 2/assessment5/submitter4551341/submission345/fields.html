Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

  • CpG islands tend to be protected from DNA methylation in normal cells, in other words, largely unmethylated. This allows the promoter to act and cause expression of the gene. The intergenic regions, repetitive elements and introns are generally methylated.
In cancer CpG islands are methylated or hyper methylated which effectively blocks the promoter and silences the expression of tumor suppressor genes, thus allowing growth, potentially uncontrolled. The intergenic and repetitive elements and introns are hypo methylated in cancer, a swap of methylation from normal cells. 
Cancer can be caused by activation of oncogenes genetically, or by inactivation of tumor suppressors by epigenetically silencing. The benefit of the cause being of epigenetic origin is that epimutations are reversible.
There are two types of DNA methylation abnormalities: Hypermethylated CpG islands (CGI's) and Hypo methylated intergenic regions, repetitive elements, and introns.

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.
The imprint control region is methylated on the paternal allele in the H19/Igf2 cluster. H19 is silenced.This allows the enhancers to act on Igf2 and the gene is expressed. On the maternal allele the ICR, imprint control region is unmethylated, the enhancers act on H19 to promote expression while Igf2 is silent.
Imprinting is disrupted by hyper methylation of the CpG islands in the ICR.
With loss of imprinting Igf2 is expressed from both allele's, a double dose of Igf2, a gene associated with growth, resulting in Wilm's tumor.

Decitabine is in the class of DNA demethylating agents.  It apparently helps to demethylate and allow expression where there had been previous silencing,and possibly slow tumor growth. Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.
DNA methylation is an epigenetic mark that affects the expression of the genes, including tumor suppressor genes.
A sensitive period is when epigenetic  marks are being established, during germ cell development and early embryonic development.
Studies have indicated there may be a "grandmother effect", where environmental changes, diet, possibly drugs used, during pregnancy can affect not only the mid-gestation fetus but also their germ cells which are being developed for the next generation.
Since epigenetic marks are mitotically heritable, changes in the DNA methylation can affect more than one generation. Treating patients during sensitive periods may have consequences in future generations.
