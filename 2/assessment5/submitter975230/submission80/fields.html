Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

In a normal cell, the CpG islands are hypomethyllated (majority of the bases are not methyllated), and the intergenic regions and the repetitive elements are usually hypermethyllated. However, in a cancer cell, this is usually the other way round (CpG islands are hypermethyllated, and the intergenic regions and the repetitive elements are hypomethyllated. DNA methyllation is a way to silence tumor supressive genes, so if the CpG island is methyllatedad, that can silence the tumor supressor genes, which contribute to the forming of the cancer. What is more, hypomethyllated intergenic regions and repetitive elements can cause further disruption of the normal cell cycle, becaus they are not silenced, and for eg long non-codig RNA transcription can occur more often. Because methyllation is mitotically heritable, the epimutations are rapidly selected, thus contributing to the spreading of the mutated epigenetic marks.
Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.
The H19/Igf2 cluster is a subject to imprinting. Normally, the Igf2 gene from the paternal allele is expressed, while the same gene from the maternal allele is suressed. The H19 when methyllated (paternal allele) is not active, so the CTCF molecule that is regulated by the regions that can be found on the DNA upstream from the H19 location, are silenced as well. CTCF regulates the expression if Igf2, so on the paternal allele, because there is no CTCF to silence the Igf2 gene, it is expressed. However, on the maternal allele, the H19 is hypomethyllated, so it does not silence the CTCF complex, which in return silences the expression of the Igf2 gene that is on the maternal allele. In Wilm's tumor, both H19 genes are methyllated, which causes both Igf2 genes to be espressed, so the imprinting no longer is in place. Igf2 is a growth promoting gene, so this means double dose of a growth hormone, enhancing the growth rate of the cancer cells.
