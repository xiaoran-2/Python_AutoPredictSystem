Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

DNA methylation is context dependent and also has different patterns depending on the cancer type, so we can say methylation is cancer specific.In general terms DNA methylation in cancer cell  is find in the opposite situation respecting to the normal cell : in the case of intergenic regions and repetitive elements, which If they were methylated in a normal cell in order to provide genome stability, we find them hypomethylated in cancer cell to provoque inestability. Also hypomethylation/ hypermethylation will be switched into the contrary function in order to activate those genes who had to be silenced ( oncogenes), or to silence those genes which should be activated ( tumor supressor genes).
  • DNA methylation at CpG islands is a mechanism to silence gene expression because these regions used to be at promoters of the genes.
  • In cancer CpG islands are hypermethylated.
  • When hypermethylation of Cpg island targets tumor supressor genes, these are no longer expressed and initiate cancer development. This is mitotically heritable so that the numer of cells with tumor supressor gene silenced increases with time.
  • Intergenic regions and repetitive elements are usually methylated, in order to mantain genomic integrity preventing delection, insertions, duplications, reciprocal translocations, ilegitimate recombination... etc.
  •  In cancer, opposite occurs, these regions are hypomethylated so that there is genome inestability.
  • Hypomethylation in intergenic regions produces deletions, reciprocal translocations and insertions. Also hypomethylation of repetitive elements, such as CpG islands makes them to be poor promoters so that are gene activator regions.

  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour.

Imprinting is associated with methylation, and this proccess is controlled by ICR, so that this permits the monoallelic expression of one gene, that can be paternal or maternal. In cancer cells this pattern is no longer showed so there can be either a sobreexpression ( meaning both parental genes are expressed) or a diminuition where any of them is expressed.
  • Paternal allele for Igf2 that is a growth factor, is methylated, so is expressed.
  • Maternal allele for Igf2 is unmethylated, so no expressed.
  • Hypermethylation or hypomethylation, depending on the gene function are signals of lossing imprinting. In this case we have umethylation as a silencing control, so our dissorder for Wilm's tumor is going to be the opposite: hypermethylation for an overexpression.
  • When it is unmethylated CTCF binds its insulator element, and it means that the enhancers will act on H19. But if we have loss the imprinting, this is hypermethylated and so CTCF is not bind and no longer inhibiting Igf2, so is expressed also in maternal allele. We have then both paternal and maternal allele expressed and a over- signaling for growth.
