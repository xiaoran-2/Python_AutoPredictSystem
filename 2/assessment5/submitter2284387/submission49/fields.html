Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

DNA methylation is altered in cancer in several ways.  

In CpG Islands: 

  • In the normal situation methylation at CpG islands leads to silencing of the gene. In the normal situation tumour suppressor genes are unmethylated and they are active.
  • In cancer CpG islands of tumour suppressor genes are hypermethylated, silenced.
  • DNA methylation at CpG islands leads to silencing of the tumour suppressor gene. DNA methylation is mitotically heritable and epimutations are rapidly selected. So the number of cells with silenced tumour suppressor genes at the cancer focus rapidly increases.

In intergenic regions and repetitive elements:   

  • In the normal situation repetitive elements and intergenic regions are methylated leading to genomic stability.
  • In cancer cells intergenic regions and repetitive elements are hypomethylated which leads to genomic instability.
  • Hypomethylation of repeats/intergenic intervals leads to genomic instability: Illegitimate recombination between repeats, Activation of repeats and transposition, Activation of cryptic promoters and disruption to neighbouring genes can occur.
DNA methyletion leads to genomic instability and silencing of tumour suppressor genes.DNa methylation is mitotically heritable and epimutations are rapidly selected so the number of cells with altered DNA methylation profile at cancer site rapidly increases. DNA methylation can be one of the hits in the Knudsen hypothesis and increase the chance of the occurrance of cancer.
Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

Hypermethylation of ICR’s, loss of imprinting, can contribute to cancer. For example Wilm's tumour:

Normal situation:

  • In the normal situation methylation at the ICR of the paternal allele prevents CTCF binding.  Without CTCF , DNA methylation spreads to the H19 promoter, H19 is silenced and enhancers access Igf2 to activate, Igf2 is expressed.
  • The maternal allele is unmethylated, allowing the CTCF (an insulator protein) to bind. CTCF insulates Igf2 from downstream enhancers and allow enhancers to access the  H19 promoter. H19 is expressed and Igf2 is not expressed

Wilm’s tumour, loss of imprinting; 

  • The ICR at the maternal allele is also methylated, H19 is silenced and Igf2 is expressed. Leading in the cell to a double dose of Igf2 and no H19 at all.
  • Igf2 is growth promoting. H19 expresses a non-coding RNA that functions as a tumour suppressor,, No expression of H19 in combination with double expression of Igf2  leads to Wilm’s tumour.
Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 

Decitabine is a drug used in cancer treatment that has an effect on the epigenome: 

  • Decitabine is an enzyme that belongs to the DNA MethylTransferase (DNMT) inhibitors.
  • It demethylates DNA (in low concentrations). The optimal dose for cancer treatment is rather low, it is a dose where the anti-neoplastic effect is maximal and the toxic, nonspecific effect is tolerable. 
  • DNMT inhibitors are nucleoside analogues that irreversibly bind DNMTs after they are incorporated into DNA. DNMT inhibitors, like Decitabine, are replication dependent. Decitabine affects dividing cells. A characteristic of cancer is that cancer cells divide more rapidly than normal cells and are more severely affencted by the drug, because they are replicating more. The mechanism of action of the drug is still unclear. It might work in haematological malignancies since they are dependent on tumour suppressor gene hypermethylation of the CpG islands.
Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.

Drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment.

  • DNA methylation produces a stable epigenetic mark, once it is established it is normally only removed at a period of epigenetic reprogramming in germ cells, it is maintained in somatic cells. It is mitotically heritable. Reprogramming occurs during gametogenesis and in ealy development in vivo.
  • A sensitive period in epigenetics is a period of epigenome reprogramming. At that period epigenome can receive new epigenetic marks that can stay put during the lifespan and be passed on to the progeny.
  • Sensitive periods are; early development (right after fertilization to formation of the epiblast) and Primordial Germ Cell (PGC) development; oogenesis in females (it is complete either before or shortly after birth) and spermatogenesis in males ( it starts in puberty and continues during life) extending to the period of germ cell maturation (9-12 in ♂ and 8-10 in ♀).
If you treat patients at the sensitive periods the epigenome that is changed by the drug stays active for the rest of the lifespan (leading to undesirable side effects) and can be transmitted to future generations. Treatment during these periods should be avoided as much as possible.
