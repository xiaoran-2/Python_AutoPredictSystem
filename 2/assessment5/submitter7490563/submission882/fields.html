Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

If the imprint control region is methylated on the paternal allele and not on the maternal allele, then CTCF will bind to the imprint region on the maternal allele thus acting as an insulator forcing the enhancers to act on H19 therefore making igf2 silent on the maternal allele causing an absence of expression. Because the imprint region on the paternal allele is methylated, CTCF does not bind to act as an insulator therefore the enhancers act on igf2 instead of H19. The interaction between the enhancers and igf2 then causes expression at the igf2 region.

Imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour from the loss of imprinting, hypermethylation of both the maternal and paternal ICRs, igf2 is overexpressed causing Wilm’s tumour  

 Disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer by either both paternal alleles being active (causing Wilm’s tumour due to Igf2 being growth promoting,) or both will be silenced (causing Beckwith-Wiedemann Syndrome and possible cancer)

Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

 

The normal function of DNA methylation at a CpG island is to silence the gene, this is an inactive epigenetic mark.

 DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer by a gain of silencing marks and loss of activation marks at the CPG islands

CPG islands are sometimes the shores of tumor suppressor genes that get silence not allowing the activation of tumor suppressing genes.

Intergenic regions and repetitive elements should both be methylated in normal functioning cells, this ensures the maintenance of genomic integrity.

Intergenic regions and repetitive elements are unmethylated in cancerous cells; this causes genomic instability produced by illegitimate deletions, insertions and reciprocal translocations.

Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.

epigenetic changes (like altering methylation) are passed on to daughter and granddaughter cells during cell division until they are actively erased. This allows the epigenetic changes to be affective long after administering the initial treatment

A sensitive period is a stage in cell development that is sensitive to epigenetic changes, at this stage the cell is clearing epigenetic marks to restore totipotency. There are two waves in early development, the preimplantation period and primordial germ cell development. Treatment during these periods may distort DNA methylation imprints, thus negatively effecting development.
