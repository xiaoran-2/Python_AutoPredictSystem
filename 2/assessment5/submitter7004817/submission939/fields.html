Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

DNA methylation(a heritable epigenetic mark) involves the covalent transfer of a methyl group to the C-5 position of the cytosine ring of DNA by DNA methyltransferases. CpG islands (CpGIs) are short interspersed DNA sequences that are different from the average genomic pattern by being GC-rich and maintained in a predominantly nonmethylated state and are sites for transcription initiation. Shared DNA sequence features, along with hypomethylation, adapt CpGIs for promoter function by destabilizing nucleosomes and attracting proteins that create a transcriptionally permissive(open) chromatin state (Deaton.A & Bird A. Genes and Development 2011. 25:1011). Some CGIs do become methylated in normal development - as with one female X chromosome becoming  'inactivated', whilst the CpGIs on the other (functioning) X chromosome remains nonmethylated and 'active' (see course notes).

It is proposed that CpGIs are subject to sporadic de novo methylation which is then actively removed by a DNA demethylase. Defects in such a system may predispose to residual (de novo) methylation as seem in many cancers and related to extrinsic (environmental) factors. So, a previously hypomethylated promotor (CpGI) region becomes methylated, playing an important role in oncogenesis due to its influence on local chromatin structure/ gene activity regulation, resulting in transcriptional silencing of important Tumour suppressor genes which control cell cycle, apoptosis or DNA repair .

Intergenic regions with repetitive elements (approx 45%) of the mammalian genome consists of transposable and viral elements that are silenced by bulk methylation and so allow stability of the genome. In cancer, instead there is global hypomethylation and reduced supression of repetitive element transcription and transposition, leading to genomic instability and deregulation of tissue specific and imprinted genes resulting in oncogene activation.  (Wu H, et al . 2011. Genes Dev25: 679–684). Proto-oncogenes are a group of genes that cause normal cells to become cancerous when they are mutated into ongogenes.

Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.
Imprint control region (ICR) methylation is established in primordial germ cells to achieve parent­ of ­origin specific epigenomic marks, maintained in the embryo. This ICR methylation is actively protected (genetic imprinting) during reprogramming in the early 
embryonic period, right after the egg is fertilized, when the paternal genome and  maternal genome are heavily demethylated, This occurs because the DNA has particular methylation patterns to be re-established for proper cell functioning of the sperm and the egg.  When new germ cells are formed in the embryo, these ICR regions are erased and re­methylated according to the gender. Both these times are 'SENSITIVE PERIODS' of development. Drugs used at this time (pregnant woman- very early first trimester) can dysregulate methylation and affect gene imprinting in the primordial germ cells- leading to effects that last well beyond the period of drug administration so drug administration then is inadvisable and can lead to enduring effects in the offspring (and their future offspring- notably female).
Importantly, DNA methylation is essential for normal development- involved in key processes including: genomic imprinting, X-chromosome inactivation, and suppression of repetitive element transcription and transposition. With specific genes remaining epigenetically silenced through gametogenetic reprogamming, genomic integrity is maintained. (ref:Dr.Baylin 'Economist').

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.
A small proportion (<1%) of genes in mammals are imprinted-  gene expression occurs from only one allele.The expressed allele is dependent upon its parental origin e.g: gene encoding Igf2 is only expressed from the non-imprinted paternal allele - therefore d (remains nonmethylated) in the father, while H19 is expressed from the non-imprinted (nonmethylated) maternal allele. The imprinted alleles are silenced by methylation. Therefore unlike the paternal Igf2 allele, in the mother this Igf2 allele is silenced (hypermethylation) and imprinted (non-imprinted H19  allele is nonmethylated and active). So, genomic imprinting involvies DNA methylation to achieve monoallelic gene expression (silencing of imprinted gene).

In Wilms tumours maternal IGF2 imprint is 'relaxed'-the gene is expressed biallelically with expression of this maternal IGF2 associated with no  H19 expression. Acquisition of this paternal 'epigenotype' contributes to cancer. (Proc.Natl. Acad.Sci, Vol 192. USA  2005)The 'relaxation' involves alteration in expression of imprinted genes controlled by Imprint Control Regions (ICR) involving reversing methylation of maternally inherited IGF2 (unsilencing) and allows methylatiing of H19 allele (silencing). At the H19/Igf2 cluster methylation prevents enhancer blocking by stopping CTCF binding to the ICR, Without CTCF, DNA methylation spreads to H19 promoter to silence AND enhancers can now access Igf2 to 'activate'.(course notes).

Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 

Decitabine treats certain cancers and belongs to the DNA-demethylating class of epigenetic inhibitors.  It is produced by the Japanese company Eisai and is sold under the name of "Dacogen". By inhibiting DNA methylation, Decitabine importantly impacts the DNA methylation regulation involved in disease led silencing of important genes, allowing instead their activation and expression. No longer silenced, important tumour suppressor genes can then contribute to anti-tumour activity to help 'fight' the cancer. The development of cancer is known to be associated with promotor region hypermethylation and gene silencing, in addition to adverse 'global' genonome hypomethylation.

Decitabine by causing DNA-demethylation acts as an epigenetic inhibitor of ‘gene silencing’  – removing methyl groups from the DNA  allow previously silenced (e.g: by tumour induced 'promotor region' hypermethylation) genes to now be expressed. Decitabine results in the “unsilencing” of Tumour suppressor genes and expression of their anti-tumour effect e.g to reduce tumour proliferation.(Economist article).
