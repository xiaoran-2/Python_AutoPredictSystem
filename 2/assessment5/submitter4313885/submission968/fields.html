Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

Normally, around 70-80% of CpG dinucleotides are methilated in mammalian somatic cells. Methylation of gene promoters usually correlates with silencing gene expression. Some promoters have unmethylated CpG islands, this means they are transcribed. They are hypermethylated in case of cancer, which in turn can cause silencing of those genes, which might include tumor supressor genes.
Intergenic regions and repetetive elements are usually methylated to prevent transposons from jumping and disrupting functional genes. These areas get hypomethylated during cancer and thus, transposable elements can copy themselves and insert into previously functional genes, disrupting them. Thus if a transposon lands in a gene that is responsible for suppresing tumors or unlimited growth, cancer begins to develop.

Decitabine is a DNA methyltransferase inhibitor and serves to hypomethylate DNA. If a tumor is caused by a hypermethylation of certain CpG islands, such drug, could prevent methylation of second strand of DNA after copying and mitotically dividing, restoring normal gene methylation and functioning in subsequent daughter cells, thus supressing tumor and resuming normal function.

Altering DNA methylation can cause certain genes to be expressed more strongly or silence them. This could in turn lead to lasting consequences by supressing or overexpressing genes that code proteins responsible for epigenetic modifications, such as further DNA methylation or histone modification.

Sensitive periods are such periods in which epigenetic marks, mostly methylation, is removed and established anew.
These periods are just after fertilization and during germ cell developement. 

Treating patients with such drugs during sensitive periods - i.e. female gamete developement (childhood to adolescence) could interfere with developement of those gametes and so interfere with imprinting and that is only if gametes develop at all, this could lead to infertility.
