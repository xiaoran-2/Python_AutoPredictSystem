Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 
Decitabine belongs to the class of DNA methyl transferase inhibitors.
When Decitabine is present in a cell that divides it gets incorporated
into the DNA during the replication process.  Once it is attached to
the DNA and a DNA methyl transferase (DNMT) comes along, the DNMT will
bind to Decitabine and then will no longer be able to methylate other
sites in the DNA.  It is known that certain tumors are strongly linked
to CpG island hypermethylation, so Decitabine's effect of reducing the overall
methylation (by disabling the DNMTs that methylate DNA) will reduce CpG island
hypermethylation and thus can have an anti-tumor effect.
Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

The purpose of DNA methylation at CpG islands is to silence gene
expression.  The methylation at a cytosine is established by
methyltransferase proteins DNMT3a/b and is mitotically heritable 
because during DNA replication another methyltransferase, DNMT1, 
copies the methyl group from the parent strand to the daughter strand.
The CpG islands are usually found at the promoter regions of genes and
when they are methylated, CpG binding proteins (MeCP1 and MeCP2)
can bind and prevent transcription thus silencing this gene.  In normal
cells very few CpG islands are methylated while in cancer we find
hypermethylation of various CpG islands.  If the CpG region happens 
to be at the promoter region of a tumor suppressor gene the hypermethylation
will cause the tumor suppressor gene to be silenced  and hence the cell
can grow into a tumor.

Intergenic regions and repetitive elements tend to be methylated in
normal cells for two main reasons: maintaining genomic integrity and
avoiding transcriptional interference.  Both intergenic regions and
repetitive elements contain LTR's which are very strong promoters for
proteins gag and pol.  Gag and pol facilitate copy and pasting (and cut
and pasting) within the DNA leading to a variety of genomic mutations. So
silencing them by methylating their LTR promoters is good for maintaining
genomic integrity.  Furthermore, intergenic regions and repetitive
elements contain cryptic promoters which go in the opposite direction
of regular promoters and therefore may interfere with promoters going in
the normal direction. Silencing these cryptic promoters via methylation
thus avoids transcriptional interference.  In cancer the intergenic
regions and repetitive elements tend to be hypomethylated.  The loss of
methylation means a reduction in silencing, in particular for the LTR's,
so gag and pol proteins will be expressed and will cause genomic
instabilities, i.e. mutations in the DNA that then lead to cancer.
Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.
On the paternal allele the imprint control region (ICR), which consists
of a CTCF binding site and the H19 gene, is methylated.  The methylation
prevents CTCF, which would act as an insulator, from binding.  Without
this insulator downstream transcription enhancers can act on the Igf2
gene (which is upstream from the imprint control region) and Igf2
is expressed.  One the maternal allele the ICR is unmethylated so CTCF
can bind and act as an insulator.  The insulation prevents the enhancers
from acting on Igf2, so Igf2 will not be expressed from the maternal
allele. 

In Wilm's tumor, however, the ICR of the maternal allele is methylated
as well so Igf2 will be expressed from the maternal allele as well
leading to an overexpression of Igf2.  Since Igf2 is a growth promoting
protein its overexpression can contribute to uncontrolled cell growth,
i.e. cancer.
Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.
DNA methylation changes which genes are expressed.  Broadly speaking,
higher levels of methylation on the histone tails of a particular gene
have a silencing effect so that the DNA for this gene can no longer
be transcribed to RNA.  A sensitive period is a time where an altered
environment can have an effect on the methylation state of the DNA and
therefore can change the epigenetic control of gene expressions.
Sensitive periods in the development of mammals include the pre- and
early postimplantation of the embryo as well as the primordial germ
cell development within the embryo.  These time periods are sensitive
because during this time the cells in the embryo undergo an active
remodeling of their epigenome.  Treating pregnant women during sensitive
(for the embryo) periods is inadvisable because drugs that can alter
for example methylation patterns can disrupt the normal epigenetic
control of the embryo.
