Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

DNA methylation is the process by which enzymes called methyltransferases help to attach a methyl group to CpG islands; a region in linear DNA where cytosine and guanine are separated by one single phosphate. DNA methylation in mammals helps to turn a gene off, or in other words, silence it.

Many a time, the methyltransferases get mutated and start to methylate the histones (packaging unit of DNA) excessively. This increased amount of methylation causes too of much silencing of the genes, which may also silence the tumor-suppressing genes, which are needed for managing the uncontrolled proliferation of tumor-causing cells. This chain reaction leads to cancer. 

DNA methylation occurs intergenic regions and repetitive elements such as LINEs, SINEs, and satellite repeats as well. This may lead to repression of transcriptional noise from alternative start sites, inhibition of antisense transcription or direct RNA splicing and may relate to replication timing (Biochimie, 1994).

In cancer, DNA methylation at intergenic sites and repetitive elements is disrupted as excessive cell proliferaton demands increase in methyltransferase production which leads to increased DNA-methylation. This causes excessive silencing of genes which silence tumor suppressor genes and so, the process of exponential proliferation of cells continues and grows.

Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 

Decitabine is a kind of DNA-methylating agent, used to treat myelodysplastic syndromes which are the precursors to acute myelogenous leukemia.

The drug acts on the DNA by reducing the amount of methylation occurring in the cell, thus lowering the amount of genes being silenced. 

This drug can act in combination with deacetylases inhibitors in the treatment of cancer. They act by slowing the growth of solid tumors, by penetrating through the tumor. By decreasing the amount of DNA-methylation in the cell, silencing of tumor-suppressor genes also gets reduced. As tumor suppressors are synthesized, they help to control the rapid proliferation of cancer cells. Epigenetic changes are inherited by daughter cells and continue to be inherited through generations of cells until they are actively erased. This system helps the Decitabine to effectively eradicate cancer cells in a solid tumor.

How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.
Alteration of DNA methylation is an epigenetic approach to the treatment of cancer. Epigenetic changes are known to be passed on to daughter cells and further through several generations of cells. These changes continue to be inherited until the cancer cells no longer proliferate and the tumor is actively erased. Due to this property of epigenetics, effects of altering DNA-methylation last even after the period of drug treatment is over.
