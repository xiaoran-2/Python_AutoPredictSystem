Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

Genes are strands of material that essentially transmit information to create the phenotype of the organism. The more open and exposed these strands are, the more effect they have. Methylation essentially wraps up genes, causing them to express less of their information. "CpG" is DNA in which a cytosine nucleotide is next to a guanine nucleotide in a linear sequence of bases, and is separated by one phosphate (Cytosine+phosphate+Guanine=CpG). Most of the genes responsible for phenotype expression are found in clusters called CpG islands. Methylation is an essential part of gene activity by reducing the effect of genes that are not needed, such as the unused gene in an allele.

In cancer, genes at CpG islands can be hypermethylated, meaning they are so tightly wrapped up they are completely silenced. This process causes necessary information not to be transmitted, such as the information to tell a cell to stop growing. Cancer can also cause hypomethylation, or not methylated when they should be, causing gene information that should not have been expressed to express.

Between the CpG islands are stretches of DNA sequences called intergentic regions, or IGR. These IGRs contain a lot of repetitive information, and much of it is ordinarily methylated. Cancer typically causes hypomethylation in IGRs causing this information to be expressed. This is particularly disruptive as DNA goes through a process of recombining while replicating, and the expression of these repetitive elements confuses the process leading to DNA damage.

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

The H19 gene essentially prevents the IGF2 gene from expressing. In a process called imprinting, the paternal allele of the H19 gene is ordinarily always fully methylated, resulting in no expression. This hypermethylation of the H19 paternal allele allows for the expression of the paternal allele of the IGF2 gene. Conversely, the maternal allele of the H19 gene is ordinarily always unmethylated resulting in no expression of the maternal allele of the IGF2 gene.

In Wilm’s tumor, the maternal H19 allele is also hypermethylated. This results in the maternal IGF2 allele expressing itself.

When the imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted, it results in both the paternal and maternal IGF2 alleles expressing themselves. This leads to an overgrowth of the cell resulting in a tumor.

Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 

Decitabine is a DNA hypomethylating agent. It inhibits DNA methyltransferase, or the process that causes methylation. As hypermethylation can cause cells to grow out of control in cancer, Decitabine helps to prevent genes from being incorrectly hypermethylated.

Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.

DNA methylation is passed on as the cell divides and replicates. Once methylation is deactivated, daughter cells formed in the replication process of mitosis will not re-methylate. Therefore, stopping the incorrect hypermethylation will result in the new cells not having the cancerous properties, essentially isolating the cancer to the existing cells and stopping it from spreading.

Since drugs that affect the epigenetic process spread throughout the entire system, it is important not to use them on patients in sensitive periods. A sensitive period would include in younger patients when they are developing germ cells. These drugs essentially switch off the epigenetic process that is essential for early development, and employing these drugs during the development of germ cells might cause disruption of the critical epigenetic processes of their later embryos.
