Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

DNA methylation occurs almost exclusively at CpG dinucleotides and most CpG sequences in the genome are methylated. DNA methylation is catalyzed by a family of DNA methyltransferase (DNMT) enzymes. However, there are CpG islands associated with gene promoters that escape DNA methylation.

Active genes tend to be enriched with special changes whereas inactive genes have different specific enrichments. DNA methylation was the first epigenetic alteration to be observed in cancer cells.

Hypermethylation events on usually unmethylated CpG islands are set up in a large number of cancer genes. Hypermethylation of CpG islands at tumour suppressor genes switches off these genes, whereas global hypomethylation leads to genome instability and inappropriate activation of oncogenes and transposable elements.

DNA methylation is also the principal epigenetic factor governing allelic imprinting or the process by which only one allele of certain factors is expressed depending on the parental origin.

DNA methylation levels, which are maintained by DNMT enzymes, delicately balance within cells, and over-expression of DNMTs is linked to cancer in humans. Most genes that involve DNA methylation levels affect histone modifications. H3K9 methylation and DNA methylation are associated. Moreover, in cancer cells, disruption of DNA methylation is linked to loss of H4K20 methylation.

A gene remembers whether it was inherited from the father or the mother and is expressed accordingly.  DNA methylation is part of this memory process and loss of imprinting has been tightly linked to cancer susceptibility. For instance, the Igf2 gene is an imprinted gene that is shown only from paternally-inherited alleles. However, aberrant methylation of the Igf2 imprinting control region on the maternal allele leads to the additional expression of the maternal copy. Therefore, the points of the Igf2 rise approximately twofold and this elevated level may be linked to cancer susceptibility. 
Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

In normal cells, the paternal Igf2 and maternal H19 genes are expressed. Several sites upstream of H19 are methylated on the paternal allele and unmethylated on the maternal allele.

With loss of imprinting, the maternal chromosome reverses to a paternal epigenotype, and with a paternal pattern of methylation of the H19 promoter, Igf2 turned on, and H19 turned off, causing increased growth of tumor cells and prevent damaged cells from being destroyed.

Loss of imprinting of the Igf2 gene has been identified in cancer known as embryonal tumors, which include an uncommon variety of kidney cancer called Wilm’s tumor. Loss of imprinting of Igf2 and the tightly linked H19 locus has been associated with tumorigenesis in a diversity of patients.

H19 and Igf2 expression are closely interrelated, equally they are expressed in the same tissues during fetal development. This, coupled expression is only lost in cases of loss of imprinting.

The increased activity of the Igf2 gene has been associated with many types of cancer. In Wilms' tumor, imprinted expression of Igf2 is commonly relaxed, resulting in maternal allele expression. This is associated with increased methylation and reduced expression from the maternal copy of the linked H19 locus.

Decitabine is a drug known as DNA demethylating agents. It belongs to the category of chemotherapy called antimetabolites which are similar to normal substances within the cell. The cells contain these substances and interact with a number of targets within the cell to produce a direct cytotoxic effect that causes death of rapidly dividing cancer cells.

DNA methylation is a major mechanism that modulates gene expression in cells and an increase in DNA methylation can result in the stoppage of the bodily function of suppressor genes that govern cell division and growth, where unregulated cell division allows or promotes cancer.

Considering the anti-tumor effect, Decitabine is the DNA methyltransferase inhibitor, which works as a modifier of epigenetic profile. This modification process is followed by the interfering with the methylation of DNA to restore normal function of the tumor suppressor genes, thus restoring control over cell growth.
