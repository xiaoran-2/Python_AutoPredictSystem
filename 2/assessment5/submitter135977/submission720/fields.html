Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

In normal cells, the CpG islands function as promoters for transcription.

Normally the CpG islands are not methylated in normal cells. Methylation of the CpG island normally will silencing the gene expression.

The methylation of the CpG islands, intergenic regions and repetitive elements are mitotic heritable.

 

In Cancer cells, the CpG islands are usually methylated.

 

The methylation of CpG islands silence the expression of the gene associated to the CpG islands. This cause that e.g. enzymes produced via mRNA from the gen are not produced or produced in smaller amount. That means, that if the gen produces enzymes that suppress cell division, then the cell division will not be suppressed as it was before, that means the cell divides faster. Its daughter cells inherit the CpG methylation, they will therefore also divide faster than normal cells. The original normal cell has therefore been transformed to cancer cells.

 

In normal cells, the intergenic regions and repetitive elements are methylated.

The methylation cause the DNA string to become densely packed heterochromatin.

The densely packing cause that many genes are not expressed or only little expressed.

 

In cancer cells, the intergenic regions and repetitive elements are not methylated.

 

When the intergenic regions and repetitive elements are not methylated the DNA string becomes less packed than in normal cells,  it may even become unpacked euchromatin.

That cause the genes to become more expressed. Among the genes that become expressed are gens that increase cell division.  The daughter cells inherit the lacking methylation, they will therefore also divide faster than normal. The original cell therefore has developed to cancer cells.

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

An Imprinting Control Region (ICR) controls the expression of a gene. The expression of the gene is among other things determined by methylation.

 

On the paternal allele the ICR for Igf2r gene is methylated. The methylation pattern of the ICR for Igf2r is spread to the promoter H19. The promoter H19 is therefore silenced. The enhancers to Igf2r can therefore work, they are not blocked. The Igf2r is therefore expressed.

 

On the maternal allele, Igf2r is normally not methylated. That cause a protein CTCF to be placed between Igf2r and the promoter H19 and enhancers to Igf2r. That means, that the enhancers to Igf2r cannot work on Igf2r, therefore is Igf2r not expressed.

 

Wilm’s tumor:

The imprinting of the maternal cluster can become disrupted.

If the maternal cluster H19/Igf2r is methylated, then the isolating protein CTCF is not placed between Igf2r and the enhancers to Igf2r. This cause Igf2r to be expressed on both the maternal and paternal allele, it will therefore become overexpressed. Igf2r is a growth promoter. The overexpression will cause the cell to divide faster. The daughter cells inherit the methylation and will therefore also divide faster, the cells have developed to Wilm’s tumor.

Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 

Decitabine:

Decitabine is a DNA-demethylation drug. That is, it removes methylgroups from the DNA.

 

When methylgroups are removed the DNA becomes less compact, therefore the genes become more expressed. Among the more expressed genes are the suppressor genes that produce mRNA, that produce proteins that suppress the division of the cell. The production of proteins that suppress the proliferation of cells might therefore increase and the cancer therefore fade away.

 

Dectabine is used to treat myelodysplastic syndromes, that is the beginning of the serious illness myelogenous leukaemia.

Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.

DNA methylation as drugs:

Methylation determine how much the DNA is packed together. If it is packed heavily then few gens are expressed. By putting methylgoups different places, it is possible to determine which genes should be active and which genes should be passive. The methylation is mitotic heritable. The daughter cells therefore also have the methylations.

 

A sensitive period is a period, where the methylation development is fast.

 

There is two sensitive periods.

The first period is when the gem cells are formed: the primordial development.

The other period is the development from the zygote has been formed to epiblast. The zygote methylations are first demhytelated, thereafter the cells are methylated from blastocyst to epiblast, where the somatic cells are formed.

 

During sensitive periods a lot of methylations or demethylations have to be done. If you treat a patient with drugs that methylate or demethylate, then the drug might interfere with the natural methylation and demethylation. The drug might e.g. methylate the DNA string so that the DNA string is packed in a way, that hinder the natural methylation can be done, or the drug might set methylgroups that is later removed naturally.
