Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

Because the CpG islands are associated with the promoters of the genes, the methylation state of the CpG islands are connected to the expression of the genes. If the CpG island is methylated, the corresponding gene is silenced, if it is unmethylated the gene is expressed. In cancer cells the CpG islands within the promoters of the cancer suppressor genes are often methylated, resulting the suppression of the suppressor genes. As the suppressors are switched off, the cell easily become a cancer cell as it can be one of the
hits in the Knudson hypothesis. As the methylation is associated with the silencing, normally the intergenic regions and the repetitions are silenced by the DNA methylation. But in the cancer cells that regions are lost the methylation, leads the loss suppression. The overall methylation of the DNA is decreasing in the cancer cells as the cancer develops. The active repetitive elements cause genetic instability and increased mutation rates, as the repetitions are disrupts the normal gene pairing and separation during the mitosis.

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.
The binding site of the CTCF and the promoter of H19 gene are differently methylated. In the maternal allele, the site is not methylated allowing the binding of the CTCF, which serves an insulator protein, insulates the igf2 from the downstream enhancer and allow the expression of the H19. In contrast the in the paternal allele the site is methylated, so no CTCF binding, no insulation -> the enhancers can access the Igf2. Moreover the methylation spreads to the promoter of the H19, silencing the gene.
As the methylation of the maternal allele changed and methylated like the paternal allele, the CTCF can't bind to the binding site allows the downstream enhancers to reach the igf2 and both the maternal and paternal igf2 become expressed and both H19 are suppressed resulting an increased cell growth. And the increased cell growth can lead to cancer.
