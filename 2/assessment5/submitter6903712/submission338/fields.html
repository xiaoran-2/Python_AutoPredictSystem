Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

DNA methylation is a key concept in cell differentiation. It is simply methyl group binding to cytosine or adenine nucleotides. It is normally permanent and unidirectional mechanism. In addition to cell differentiation it is important for X-chromosome inactivation, repetitive genes suppression and also maintenance of imprinted genes and formation of heterochromatin.

CpG islands are mostly in or near promoters and usually they are kept free of methylation. Methylation of a CpG island will inactivate related gene and result is loss of function of this gene. The consequences will depends on which gene functions are lost (can be tumor or repetitive gene suppression gene). (Sometimes single gene can do same effect with mutation. (e.g. BRCA1 in breast cancer, RB in retinoblastoma) In a normal cell CpG islands are mostly unmethylated but repetitive elements, intergenic regions, introns and CpG poor promoters mostly methylated. In a cancer cell it is vice versa. Consequences of these disruptions are:

a) Shortly genomic instability (illegitimate recombination between repeats, activation of repeats and transposition, activation of cryptic promoters and disruption of neighboring genes)

 b) Expression or repression of microRNA's, LncRNA's which means global disruption in the cell epigenetic system.

c) Loss of DNA repair functions.

d) Loss of imprinting which results overdose (lack of dose) because of expression (or repression) of both parental alleles

e) Loss of growth restricting genes,

f) Over expression growth promoting genes.

In summary, disrupted methylation means disrupted gene expression or repression. This disruption triggers new disruptions.  e.g. hypomethylated intergenic region mislead enhancers’, Suppressed repetitive elements starts to be over expressed, double dose (lack of dose) occurred because of loss of imprinting causes new disruptions. 

Not only one of it but mostly their total of theese effects leads the cell to cancer and cancer also leads new disruptions.

Methyltransferases are enzymes that the keys of methylation. Despite its vital importance hypo or hyper methylation can be described as epigenetic mutation which may lead to cancer.

Epigenetic mutations are reversible and hypermethyletion can be reverse by inhibiting methyltransferases. So we call  the drugs inhibiting methyltransferases as DNA-demethylating/hypo methylating (abbreviated as DNMTi) agents.

Decitabine is a DNMTi agent and approved by FDA for use myelodysplastic syndrome.

Cancer cells dividing much more rapidly Decitabine effects on division and the way it makes it binding to methyltransferase.
