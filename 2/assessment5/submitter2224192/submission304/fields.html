Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

CpG islands (CGIs) are associated with the promoters of at least 70% of the annotated genes, including house-keeping genes. They are predominantly unmethylated in normal cells. If at all methylated, it is done so as a mechanism to keep genes in switched-off state. Thus CGI methylation is important in the maintenance and regulation of gene expression and plays important role in various cellular processes like embryonic development and chromosome stability. 

Errors in methylation could be a reason for several diseases especially various cancers. Global hypomethylation and localized hypermethylation results in genomic instability and disregulation. CpG island hypermethylation results in the silencing of promoter regions of tumour suppressor genes, thus disregulating cell cycle, DNA repair and allied processes. Accumulation of such abnormal gene expression results is aberrant cell division and metastasis, contributes to the development of cancer. 

Intergenic regions and repetitive elements are in turn predominantly highly methylated in normal cells. Normally these regions are highly methylated which is associated with maintenance of chromosome structure and genome integrity. Changes in the methylation status of the intergenic regions and the repetitive elements also contribute to cancer. Cancer cells show extensive hypomethylation of these regions thus actively contributing to the tumour progression by increasing the chances of transpositions, transcriptional interference from strong promoters, illegitimate recombinations. 

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

Imprinted genes are expressed from only one parental allele. IGF2, which stimulates general growth is expressed only from the paternal allele due to imprinting. The ICR for the IGF2/H19 locus is methylated in paternal allele. The downstream enhancers therefore enhance the expression of upstream IGF2. Whereas in the maternal allele due to the unmethylated status of the ICR, CTCF binding is enabled, thus providing a physical barrier to the downstream enhancers from reaching the IGF2 genes, thus the enhancers act upon the H19 gene. 

This methylation pattern ensures that only paternal copy of IGF2 is expressed at any given time to maintain the dosage. In Wilm’s tumour hypermethylation of the ICR results in the methylation of maternal copy too, thus preventing the binding of CTCF to the ICR. With no physical barrier the downstream enhancers can now promote the expression of the maternal copy of IGF2 along with the normal expression of the paternal allele. The effect of the overexpression of IGF2 is reflected in the growth and metabolic processes along side tumourogenesis in Wilm’s tumour.  

Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 

Decitabine, marketed as Dacogen is a cytidine analog. It belongs to the class of DNA methyltransferase inhibitors. Hypermethylation induced gene silencing plays a central role in tumourogenesis, by downregulating the expression of tumour suppressor genes. This downregulation causes dysregulation of cell proliferation, differentiation and apoptosis. Decitabine inhibits the action of DNMT thus preventing excessive methylation of the genes, thus effectively reversing the hypermethylation induced gene silencing. The regulation of cellular proliferation and sensitivity to apoptosis is restored in the cell by decitabine, thus bringing about anti-tumour effect. 

Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.

DNA methylation is mitotically heritable, therefore altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome, lasting well beyond the period of the drug administration. 

However usage of such drugs is contraindicated during the sensitive periods of development, where the DNA are highly sensitive to the changes in the environment. The sensitive periods of development start from period of primordial germ cell development to the production of gametes, and also the pre-implantation and early post-implantation periods. During these sensitive periods an active remodeling and reprogramming of the epigenome happens. Removal of the epigenetic marks and re-laying of new marks happen during this period. If a patient is treated with drugs that potentially alter DNA methylation during these periods, the reprogramming of the epigenome will be affected causing drastic effects.
