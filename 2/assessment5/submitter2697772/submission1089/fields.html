Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

In the genome, there are so-called CpG islands, CG rich regions in the promoter region of the gene. Normally, these areas are hypomethylated, so that the DNA is accessible to transcription factors, and transcription can take place. In  tumors, through DNMT's these CpG sites are hypermethylated in the promoter region. This ensures that the transcription complex can not bind and therefore, for example, tumor suppressor genes are shut down. In the normal situation, the non-coding portions of the DNA, repetitive elements and intergenic regions, are hypermethylated, which contributes to  chromosomal stability and inactivanion of transposons. In addition to hypermethylation of CpG-eilanden  found in tumor cells, a general hypomethylation occers at the repetitive elements and intergenic regions. There are indications that the demethylation o the repetitive elements and intergenic regions leads to chromosomal instability and reactivation of transposons in certain tumors, such as colon cancer, seems demethylation even a more important role to play in the development of genomic damage than the hyper-methylation of CpG islands.

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

Upon loss of imprinting the regulation of genomic imprinting is disrupted by hyper-or hypo-methylation. An example of this is loss of imprinting of the IGF2/H19-locus. Expression of IGF2 normally takes place from the paternal chromosome. On the paternal allele, the ICR of H19 is hypermethylated and the downstream enhancers take care of the expression of IGF2 located upstream. On the maternal allele the insulator protein CTCFensures the non-expression of IGF2, but H19 is well expressed. Loss of imprinting of the maternal chromosome provides increased expression of IGF2, whereby the ICR of the maternal gene also is hypermethylated and hence H19 is silenced and IGF2 is expressed in both alleles, thus overexpression of IGF2, which plays an important role, which in the development of Wilm's tumor.
