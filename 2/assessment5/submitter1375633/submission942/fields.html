Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

CpG islands reside in the promoter regions of particular genes and play a protective role by remaining unmethylated, which allows expression of those genes.   In cancers, however, disruption of the normal state in a cell allows hypermethylation of the CpG islands in promoters of genes known as tumor suppressor genes in that cell.  In a cancer state, genes involved with tumor-associated processes such as cell division (mitosis), cell differentiation and extracellular matrix remodeling (organ homeostasis), or apoptosis (cell death) are repressed by methylation of the CpG islands in the promoters.  After an insult to a cell that would normally trigger death, in cancer the cell continues to live and grow beyond normal limits.

Methylation of the CpG islands prevents transcription of the gene by setting up a physical barrier to the transcriptional assembly proteins needed to bind to the promoter region.  Methylation also recruits additional proteins which modify histone and chromatin causing formation of the compacted heterochromatin to further prevent the gene from coming in contact with transcriptional proteins.

Normally, intergenic regions and repetitive elements are maintained in a methylated state within cells. Intergenic regions have the capacity to be transcribed in both directions as well as from either strand, resulting in confounding signals.  In a cancerous state, demethylation of these regions results in uncontrolled DNA transcription.  Repetitive sequences make up a large proportion of the mammalian genome.  They are involved in many aspects of the chromosome.  They are in centromeres and telomeres.  They are the long terminal repeats that are used by viruses to insinuate into the genome. And they are the transposable elements that can change position within the genome.   Maintaining methylation of these regions keeps them inactive.  Demethylation results in global changes, increased mutation rates, and chromosome instability.

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

A well-documented example of imprinting is the H19/IGF2 cluster.  IGF2 is a growth factor and is expressed from the paternal allele. H19 is transcribed from the maternal allele.  Both genes share common regulators of expression, such as enhancers and insulator protein CTCF-binding sites.  CTCF determines which gene (H19 or IGF2) the enhancer influences, and that decision is based on methylation of the imprint control region (ICR). The ICR contains the CTCF-binding regions.

 In the paternal allele the ICR is methylated which prevents H19 from being expressed.  The enhancers can work on paternal IGF2 to allow transcription.  In the maternal allele, however, the ICR is unmethylated so the CTCF insulator protein can bind which prevents the enhancers from working on maternal IGF2. Maternal H19 will be expressed. 

Wilm’s tumor occurs when the maternal allele has the ICR methylation pattern of a paternal allele so that IGF2 is expressed from both alleles.  This causes increased cell growth.

Several other cancers are the result of dysregulation of IGF2. Hypermethylation results in overexpression of IGF2. H19 may act as a tumor suppressor such that, with inactivation of H19, IGF2 will over-express.

Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 

Decitabine is a DNA methyltransferase inhibitor used to treat myelodysplastic syndromes (MDS).  These are bone marrow disorders and patients are not able to make sufficient blood cells.  Many genes are hypermethylated in MDS, for example the tumor suppressor p15INK4B.  Epigenetic silencing of p15INK4B is common in many blood disorders.

Decitabine works as a cytosine analogue, to incorporate into DNA with cell division.  Demethylation of DNA and cell differentiation allows normal expression of the tumor suppressor genes.  

Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.

We know that epigenetic changes are passed on to subsequent generations.  Effects on an individual (environmental toxins, disease, developmental insults) will alter DNA methylation.  These alterations will be present in the organism throughout its life, and the effects can be felt in subsequent generations.

During early embryonic development and gamete development are particularly sensitive periods for epigenetic changes because these are times when stripping and relaying of epigenetic marks occur. Drugs, treatments, or manipulations at these times can possibly, detrimentally, alter the natural processes of clearing and re-applying the marks will then have lasting effects on the organism.
