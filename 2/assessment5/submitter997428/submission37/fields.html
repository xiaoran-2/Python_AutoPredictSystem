Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

Normally CpG islands are mostly not methylated (are hypomethylated).  Methylation of CpG islands seems to have the function of silencing  the gene.   Methylated CpGs are also associated with chromatin condensation caused by binding proteins (MeCP1, MeCP2).  Hence with the normal hypomethylation, the genes are usually expressed.

 

Cancer changes the normal hypomethylation of CpG islands (generally in the promoters of tumour suppressor genes) to hypermethylation, thus inactivating these genes and allowing the cancer cells to proliferate.  The rapid proliferation  of cancer cells favours selection.  Since DNA methylation is mitotically heritable the epigenetic silencing is effectively locked in; the extent of hypermethylation increases with the proliferation of the cancer.

 

Intergenic regions and repetitive regions are normally methylated.  The function of DNA methylation at intergenic regions is to maintain genomic integrity by maintaining genomic stability and silencing transcription start sites and cryptic splice sites.    

 

At repetitive elements, methylation  maintains genomic integrity by silencing repeats and mutation of the repeats (meC to Thymine by deamination); both prevent transposition.  Silencing repeats also avoids transcription from strong promoters. Illegitimate recombination is prevented by methylation.   The Bestor genome defence model suggests that methylation at repeats protects the genome from transposable elements.

 

In contrast to CpG islands with cancer, repetitive elements and intergenic regions become hypomethylated with cancer.  This leads to genomic instability, manifested illegitimate recombination between repeats, repeats are activated and transposed, cryptic promoters are activated and neighbouring genes disrupted.





Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

 

Normally the paternal allele is paternally imprinted, i.e. it is methylated at the imprint control region (ICR), as is H19 due to heterochromatin or DNA methylation spreading, allowing the downstream enhancers to promote Igf2 expression.

 

The ICR of the maternal allele on the other hand is unmethylated, allowing CTCF and its cofactors to bind its insulator element.  The enhancers are blocked from acting on Igf2, so H19 is promoted by the enhancers. (Igf2 is silent – not expressed.)

 

Hence, Igf2’s expression is normally promoted from the paternal allele and not the maternal allele.

 

With loss of imprinting, the ICR on the maternal allele is also methylated, resulting in enhancement of the expression of Igf2 on the maternal allele.   So, both alleles express Igf2, instead of just the paternal allele (i.e. overexpression).   Igf2 is growth promoting (it is an oncogene), and this growth proliferation contributes to a childhood cancerous growth starting in the kidney called Wilms’ tumour.  (In accordance with Knudsen, other ‘hits” may be required.)

 

Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 

Decitabine is a DNA methyltransferase inhibitor. 

 

It causes DNA-demethylation, i.e. hypomethylation of DNA, during cell division.

 

It gets inserted in the DNA on replication.  The methyltransferase  that wants to bind the nucleotide (during replication) is itself bound by the Decitabine and is unable to bind the nucleotide or transfer the methylation to the daughter strand.  Since it acts during replication, non-dividing cells are relatively insensitive to Decitabine, but has major effects on rapidly dividing cancer cells.

 

It is currently used at very low doses; at high doses it is cytotoxic, causing cell death. 

 

 




Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.

The DNA methylation marks are mitotically heritable and hence persist, even for the life of the organism.

 

A sensitive period is a time when the environment may be able to influence epigenetic makeup.   Exposure e.g to tobacco smoke may be particularly deleterious to the developing organism, and in fact future generations in the case of germ cell development.  

 

Generally, the time of primordinal germ cell development to the production of mature egg and sperm (i.e to the young adult stage) and early embryo development (pre-implantation and early post-implantation) are regarded as the two most sensitive periods in the mammalian life cycle. Note that epigenetic reprogramming occurs at these times. 


In rats, maternal licking of pups leads to less stressed adults by setting the appropriate epigenetic marks in the hippocampus.  Similarly, children in loving families also usually grow up well adjusted.  Could this also be epigenetic?

 

Anything that would interfere with the epigenetic machinery could have (currently) unknown and probably deleterious effects on the organism and future generations during these two sensitive periods .  So, drugs that target the genome or epigenetics (e.g. methylation state) should be administered (if at all) with great caution, and in very much lower doses than normal.
