  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

DNA is methylated especially at intera-genic and near repetitive segements, while it is not methylated at CpG islands upstreem the promotors of tumor suppressor genes. Such pattern helps to transcription machinery to identify the correct promotor sequence, as unmethylated CpG islands guide and attract it to the correct position. Moreover, the intera-genic initiating sequences remains silent as well as repetitive elements.
In cancers, the methylation pattern go through a reverse pattern; thus, the CpG islands become hypermethilated while the intra-genic and repetitive elements lose their methylation. Therefore, the normal transcription decrease and the tumor suppressor gene under-express, while promotors at aberrant locations become sctive. So the transcription machinery goes out of control and start to express unwanted genes.

Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.
Epigenetic marks will pass through mitotic cell divisions to the next generations of the cells.
Thus, alterations in the DNA methylation pattern will remain the same in the clone of cells that originate from a single mother. Therefore, the effect of epigenetic drugs will last even beyond their prescription period.
By altering the epigenome, the rest of daughter cells will re-gain some of their anti-cancer activity that has been disrupted by epigentic changes in their mother cell, due to methylated CpG islands or methylated intera-genic segments and repetitive elements. 
However, there are sensitive period that epigenetic marks are erased, such as gametogenesis or before implantation of embryo, and soon re-establish for promoting differentiation and maturation of subsequent cells. During this period, interfering with epigenome will be harmful and should be avoided.
