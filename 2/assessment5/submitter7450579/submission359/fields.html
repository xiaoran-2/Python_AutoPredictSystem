Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 
Decitabine is an DNMT (DNA methyltransferase) inhibitor. It irreversible attaches the methyltransferase to the daughter DNA strand when a cell is replicating and therefore methylation is not transmitted to the daughter cell and methyltransferases become less and less. The effect on cancer cells is, that those are fast reproducing cells and therefore will be affected the most if DNA methyltransferases are missing and cannot be used anymore in DNA methylation.
Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

In normal cells CpG islands are predominantly de- or hypomethylated due to the fact that they often contain the promoters of gens. However in cancer cells CpG are becoming hypermethylated. This often goes along with the silencing of tumor suppressor genes.
A similar swap in methylation can be seen in intergenic regions and repetitive elements. However these areas are hypermethylated in normal cells and become more demethylated in cancer cells. This hypomethylation of the genome works in favor of cancer cells due to the progressing genome instability that especially comes along with open euchromation that is due to unmethylated repetitive areas. In case of intergenic regions cryptic promoters tend to be more active in cancer cells due to the lower level of methylation, too. This often goes along with higher expression of tumor inducing factors.
Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

The function of imprinting is that certain genes are silent only on the maternal or paternal chromosome. In case of Igf2 the imprint control region (ICR) is methylated in the paternal allele which leaves Igf2 to be expressed due to enhancers downstream of the ICR; whereas in the maternal allele, where the ICR is unmethylated, those enhancers influence the also maternally unmethylated H19 gene downstream Igf2. Due to this, the enhancers do not interact with Igf2 and Igf2 is therefore not expressed.
In summary this means, that in case of Igf2 only the paternal allele is expressed in a normal cell.
However, if imprinting is disrupted in this cluster, which means the ICR is methylated on both chromosomes, H19 is not expressed at all while Igf2 is overexpressed. This leads to an overdose of Igf2 and a so called Wilm's tumor can be caused by it.



Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.

DNA methylation is passed on to daughter cell. This means that an alteration of the epigenome in form of methylation or demethylation will be passed on to daughter cells until they are actively erased or reestablished. This can be after multiple cell divisions or never at all again.
In sensitive periods where certain methylation patterns are vital, such an effect caused by a drug could be very destructive. There are multiple sensitive periods such as in organ development or embryo implantation etc. The must impactful however would be when Germ cells are formed in an embryo or when egg or sperm cells are developing in adults. These are the time points where a wrong methylation could be fixed to the germ line and passed on to future generations. Therefore pregnant woman and especially children before and during puberty should not be treated with drugs that might have an impact on these sensitive periods.
