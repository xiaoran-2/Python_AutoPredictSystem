Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 
Decitabine is an FDA approved drug indicated for the treatment of myelodysplastic syndrome, a hematological malignancy where myeloid (blood-forming) cells are damaged. Myelodysplastic syndrome can progress to acute myeloid leukemia.

Decitabine treats cancer by functioning as a DNA methyltransferase inhibitor (DNMTi). As a nucleoside analogue, it is incorporated into DNA during replication and DNA methyltransferase binds to it. Decitabine then inhibits its activity by causing demethylation, making DNA methyltransferase irreversible. The cancer cells stop growing because Decitabine adversely affects the way that cell regulatory proteins (tumor suppressor genes) are able to bind to DNA methyltransferase. DNMT inhibitors have a greater effect on cancer cells since they replicate more than normal cells. But since Decitabine is NOT a targeted therapy, and is essentially toxic to all the cells (including normal cells), it is more effective to induce de-methylation at lower doses to have an anti-tumor effect. In this way, Decitabine can also effectively reactivate epigenetically silent tumor suppressor genes, so that normal blood count is restored.
 
Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

Cancer is a genetic disease that's caused not only by genetic mutations, but also by disturbances in the individual’s epigenome due to aberrations in DNA methylation. DNA methylation is maintained by a DNA methyltransferase enzyme known as DNMT1, and in particular instances, DNMT1 is needed for genomic stability.

 CpG islands’ normal function is to influence chromatin structure, and simplify regulation of gene expression. CpG islands are generally unmethylated in normal cells.  In particular, CpG islands are found in or near the promoter regions of tumor suppressor genes. These genes control the rate at which cells divide. When unmethylated, CpG islands allow transcription factors to bind to these promoter regions. 

Since DNA methylation is an inactive epigenetic mark, if the CpG islands become methylated, transcription factors cannot bind; these tumor suppressor genes are then silenced, and their inactivity will lead to uncontrollable cell growth. Cancer is marked by hypermethylation of CpG islands which results in a loss of function of tumor suppressor genes.

 For example, CpG island methylator phenotype (CIMP) is a tumor type that's quite frequently observed in colorectal cancers (including hereditary non-polyposis colorectal cancer), where there is hypermethylation of the promoter of the MLH1 gene (a DNA repair gene). In these types of cancers, there is an epimutation of MLH1, because it is silenced by CpG island hypermethylation.

But while DNA methylation rarely occurs at CpG islands in normal cells, it mostly occurs at repetitive elements and intergenic regions in order to protect the genome from instability. Hypomethylation at repetitive elements and intergenic regions creates transposition,  transcription of neighboring genes, illegitimate recombinations between repeats, and  transcriptional interference.  In normal cells which have DNMT1, (and hence heterochromatin), DNA methylation serves to silence these repeats and intergenic regions, so that normal cells do not become cancerous. Cancer is detailed by euchromatin, where chromosomal deletions, insertions of pieces of chromosomes, duplications of whole chromosomes (copy number variations), gene amplification, and reciprocal translocations take place between chromosomes as a result of global-wide hypomethylation.

 

 

 

 

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

Imprinted genes are originally found in the placenta, and many are involved in controlling growth in embryonic tissue, and in preventing cancer. These genes are expressed in a parent-of-origin specific manner. This mono-allelic expression is disrupted in childhood cancers by alterations in DNA methylation at ICRs (imprint control regions).

 At the H19/IGf2 cluster on chromosome 11, IGf2 is an imprinted gene that is expressed only from the paternal allele.  H19 is always expressed from the maternal allele because its ICR has a CTCF insulator binding region that is un-methylated; this un-methylated state allows this insulator protein to bind, enabling only expression of HI9, and blocking the expression of IGf2 by downstream enhancers.

On the paternal allele, DNA methylation at the paternal ICR prevents the CTCF insulator protein from binding, so that downstream enhancers are free to turn on the paternal IGf2 promoter, and so IGf2 is always expressed. DNA methylation allows heterochromatin to spread to the nearby promoter of H19, so H19 is always silenced.

The importance of DNA methylation in governing allelic imprinting is seen in Wilm’s tumor, where there is a loss of imprinting of IGf2, because the maternal ICR becomes methylated just like the paternal ICR, resulting in an overexpression of IGf2. IGf2 is growth-promoting, so when it is overexpressed, this leads to overgrowth/increased risk of cancer.

Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.

A drug which targets an enzymatic epigenetic regulator would disrupt the individual’s epigenetic state by having a long-term effect on periods of epigenetic reprogramming. This is what is termed sensitive periods, because this is when there is clearing and resetting of epigenetic marks, which are then maintained for the lifetime of the organism.

It would therefore be inadvisable to administer Decitabine to younger female patients: during the period of childhood through puberty (considering the germ line), including any period pre-conception/conception. This is because Decitabine is already a DNA methyltransferase inhibitor, so any remaining cells void of DNMT1 would be further demethylated. 

After primordial germ cell development, oocytes undergo resetting at a different rate than spermatogenesis because they mature a little later than sperm, in terms of lifespan. Oocytes remain in a haploid demethylated state until puberty. If Decitabine is administered, it would alter oocyte production, as well as alter expression of maternal effect proteins which are needed to bind to imprint control regions in the placenta, in order to maintain methylation of imprinted genes through early embryonic development.

If Decitabine is administered pre-conception, the second long-term effect would be seen during the first phase of epigenetic reprogramming (early post-fertilization), where the maternal genome becomes passively demethylated, because there's replication taking place independent of DNMT1, which is excluded from the nucleus.
