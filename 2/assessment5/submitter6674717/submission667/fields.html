Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

CpG islands (CGIs) are CG rich regions in promoters of a gene, that are responsible for controlling the expression of a particular gene. If the cytosine of the CG repeats are methylated it leads to silencing of that gene. It means that in a cell that does not require a particular protein for its function, it will methylate the CpG islands present in the promoters of all those genes thus silencing them.

All the terminally differentiated cells in an organ express only those genes whose proteins are required for the function of the cell. Genes for replication and cell division are silenced by CpG methylation. Thus such cells can only perform their specific functions and after a specific time periods initiate apoptotic pathways and are eliminated from the organs. These cells are replaced by new terminally differentiated cells arising from the organs’ stem cells. If hypo-methylation occurs at the promoters of the genes coding for proteins required for cell proliferation such proteins will get expressed in the cell and the cell will start replication and cell division leading to initiation of cancer. Also in normal cells there are proteins that are expressed that are involved in tumor suppression. If promoters of such genes get methylated at CGIs their expression is prevented leading to cell proliferation. Thus hypo-methylation of some CpG islands and hyper-methylation of others leads to uncontrolled cell proliferation i.e. cancer.

The intra-geneic repeat regions that do not code for genes also are methylated so that they do not involve in recombination processes due to sequence homologies between the chromosomes. Thus in a normal cell chromosomal aberrations such as deletion, insertions and recombinations are prevented. If hypomethylation of such intra geneic regions occur, chromosomal abnormalities can occur because of transposons and other repeats, leading eventually to cancer.


Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

Genomic imprinting is expression of some genes from only specific alles, i.e either maternal or paternal but not both. This is seen mostly in genes coding for growth factors that bring about cell proliferation. Since if both the genes are active it might lead to excessive expression of the protein and uncontroled cell proliferation.

Igf2 is one such gene that codes for insulin like growth factor. Igf2/H19 locus is a locus on chromosome 11. H19 is unmethylated on maternal copy and hence is expressed leading to expression of CTCF protein that binds to enhancer proteins and prevents them from binding to IGF2 promoter. H19 gene on the paternal allele is methylated hence the CTCF protein is also not expressed and doesnot quench the enhancer proteins. The enhancer proteins are free to initiate IGF2 protein synthesis by binding to its promoter. Thus when H19 is methylated IGF2 protein is synthesized.

In Willm's tumor there is hyper-methylation of meternal H19 gene and hence it is not active both in paternal and maternal allele. This leads to expression of IGF2 protein from both these allels. Since IGF2 is a growth factor cell proliferation is enhanced leading to cancer.
