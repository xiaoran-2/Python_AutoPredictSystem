Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

In cancer, the disruption of DNA methylation is mainly detected as genome-wide hypomethylation or tumor suppressor hypermethylation.

In normal, non-malignant cell, CpG islands represent clusters of unmethylated CpG dinucleotides that are usually found from the 5’ regulatory regions of the genes.

In cancer, the CpG islands locating at gene promoter or control regions are often hypermethylated which leads to transcriptional silencing.

The hypermethylation of transcriptional control regions leading to the silencing of tumorsuppressor genes can thus act as an alternative to a silencing mutation. The gene silencing again can lead to uncontrolled and increased rate of cell divisions or decreased rate of apoptosis.

The CpG dinucleotides in intragenic regions and repetitive elements are in normal situation methylated. It has been speculated to reflect for example the removal of transcriptional noise from alternative start sites of a gene.

In cancer, the normally methylated intragenic regions and repetitive elements are, however, non-methylated or in other words hypomethylated.

One of the main functions of DNA methylation is to maintain genomic stability. The hypomethylation of intragenic regions and repeats reduce the genomic stability by opening up the DNA and enabling recombination between chromosomes and thus increasing the risk of karyotypic abnormalities to emerge.

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

The paternal allele of H19/Igf2 is normally methylated at H19 leading to the silencing of the H19 gene. Futrthermore, the insulator protein CTCF cannot bind and the enhancers from distant promoter region of the Igf12 gene can act and activate Igf2 gene.

The maternal allele is hypomethylated at the imprinting control region (ICR) of the H19/Igf2 allowing the expression of H19 gene and the binding of CTCF insulator protein upstream of H19. This inhibits the distant enhancers to act on Igf2 promoter and thus blocks the activity of Igf2 gene. 

In Wilms tumor, both maternal and paternal alleles are hypermethylated at the imprinting control region of the H19/Igf2. This leads to paternal expression pattern from both alleles and overexpression of Igf2 gene.

Igf2 gene encodes insulin-like growth factor II which normally supports normal cell growth. The loss of imprinting leading to double dose of Igf2 leads to increase of proliferative signals and thus promotes cancer progression.

Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 
Decitabine belongs to a group of DNA-demethylating agents (DNA-methyltransferase inhibitors). It inhibits DNA methyltransferase and that way hypomethylates DNA. It is a nucleoside analog that gets incorporated into DNA during replication. Anti-tumor effect of Decitabine is based on the fact that DNA-methyltransferase binds irreversibly to the inhibitor especially on hypermethylated tumorsuppressor genes that are thus inactive. Hypomethylation restores the normal function of tumorsuppressor gene and normal growth control.
