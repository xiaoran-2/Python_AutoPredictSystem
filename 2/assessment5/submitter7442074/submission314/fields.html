Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

- Most of the CpG islands are demethylated in normal cells ensuring that the genes located at the locus are transcribed. The methyl groups on CpG islands prevent binding of transcribtion factors to the promoter leading to inhibited gene expression at these sites.
- Cancer is associated with an global hypomethylation resulting in an increased instability. Some specific gene promoters are hypermethylated leading to decreased transcription of tumor suppressor genes. 
- The disruption in CpG island methylation leads to genomic instability and abolishing of the control in essential processes including cell repair, cell division and prober differentiation leading to uncontrolled differentiation and mutagenesis. 
- In intergenic and repetitive element methyl groups are securing that transcription is kept low to prevent genomic instability 
- The methyl groups are lost, due to the global hypomethylation pattern seen in relation to cancer
- Disruption of DNA methylation at these elements will provoke deletions, translocations and insertions which contribute to tumorigenesis
Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.
- at the paternal allele H19 is not expressed since methylation of ICR prevent the binding of CTCF and thus inhibiting the enhancers to the this region. Igf2 is expressed since the enhancers are mediating the transcription of this gene site
- the maternal ICR is not methylated allowing the binding of CTCF which blocks for the activity of the enhancer on the Igf2 promoter, preventing the expression of Igf2
- In Wilm's tumor the maternal allele is expressing the Igf2 just like the paternal 
- When the Igf2 is transcribed and thus produced in excess it provokes uncontrolled cell division leading to tumorigenesis
