Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

In 60% of the promoter regions there are high density island of CpG. DNA methylation of promoter region correlates inversely with gene expression. That means DNA methylation of them results in silencing the gene. In a normal gene the CpG island must be free of methylation no matter of their state of activity.
In a cancer cell the CpG island of the promoter region become hypermethylated which generates silencing the gene. If the gene is a tumor-supressor, silencing will generate uncontrolled proliferation of the cells.
Also unusually high frequency of CpG methylation (named CIMP) generates microsatellite instability and genetic mutation founded in colorectal cancer, brest, glioma.
Another type of aberrant methylation in the colorectal cancer and nerve sheath tumor is CpG island shores which is related with gene expression.

In a normal cell, in highly expressed genes was founded methylation at intragenic region and repetitive sequences such as satellite repeats. A possible explanation  was that may repress the transcriptional noise generated in alternative site starts or inhibit antisense transcription.
A direct hypomethylation in the repetitive region was detected in malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumors. The variation in wide-genome methylation, gain or loss, generates genome instability , deregulation of tissues , imprinting genes, apoptosis and  DNA repair.









Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.
Genes imprinting is achived by methylation.
It was discovered that the Igf2  is inherited as an imprinted gene expressed on paternally allele, while H19 another imprinted gene is expressed on the maternally allele.
In the study of Igf2 gene expression it was discovered a region upstream of the gene that is more methylated on the paternal chromosome than the maternal. The loci more close to 5' to this region were highly methylated, whereas loci closely to 3'  were almost entirely unmethylated. Paternal allelic methylation in the 3' part of the Igf2 gene was found correlated with expression.
Likewise, in the neighbouring of imprinted H19 gene similar process was discovered but in an inverse manner that are more methylated on the paternal (repressed) allele than on the maternal allele.

In  Wilms tumors was discovered the maternal Igf2 imprint is relaxed thus the gene is expressed biallelically and that is correlated with low level of H19 expression, and a reversal in the patterns of DNA methylation of the maternally inherited Igf2 and H19 genes.
Hl9 appears to be an unusual class of tumor suppressor gene, which is silenced in some Wilms tumors by DNA methylation.
