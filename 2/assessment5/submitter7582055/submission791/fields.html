Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

  • DNA methylation at CpG islands:
Normal: not methylated. CpG islands are often at promoters of genes and they are normally protected from methylation. Tumor suppressor genes are active because CpG islands are in promoters of those genes -- they are doing their job.
In cancer: becomes hypermethylated. Methylation of CpG islands is mitotically heritable and is getting higher with time of cancer-genesis. Tumor suppressor genes become inactive because of hypermethylation of CpG islands. They are not functioning(stop controlling cell cycle and apoptosis) and that is one of the 'hits' in cancer initiation. 
  • DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements:
Normal: methylated. Intergenic methylation is found at repetitive sequences (satellite repeats, LINEs and SINEs). Methylation of intergentic regions and repetitive elements is very important because it helps to maintain genomic integrity. Methylation of these sites normally repress transcriptional noise from alternative start sites, inhibit antisense transcription or direct RNA splicing, also is assotiated with replication timing
In cancer: becomes hypomethylated. This change of methylations could determine genome instability (more chromosomal mutations occur), which would lead to cancer development.
Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.
  • In paternal allele of normal cells ICR (imprint control region) is methylated, H19 is methylated also (no expression) and CTCF can't bind ICR element. That's why enhancers can act directly on Igf2 and promote it's expression. 
  • In maternal allele of normal cells ICR (imprint control region) is unmethyated and CTCF can bind it. So the enhancers in this case act on H19 but there is no expression of Igf2 in this allele. 
  • Wilm's tumor: There is hypermethylation of ICR not only in the paternal but also in the maternal allele. In that way the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted, because there is no expression of H19 and also Igf2 is over-expressed (from both-parental and maternal-alleles).
  • If there is a disruption of imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster, like described before, there would be double Igf2 dose. While Igf2 is a growth promoting gene, that would lead to tumor growth -- which is necessary for cancer development.
