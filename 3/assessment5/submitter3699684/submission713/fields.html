Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

DNA Methylation at CpG islands: Normally the CpG islands present in promoter region of essential genes are hypomethylated. That means, the gene is being expressed. But in cancer cells these regions may become hypermethylated, especially in the promoters of tumour suppressor genes, so that they are silenced.

 

           When the CpG islands are hypermethylated many important genes involved in maintaining the normal cell division, like the cell cycle regulators, are not expressed, and hence the cell divides without check leading to onset of cancer.

 

DNA Methylation in Intergenic regions and Repetitive elements: Normally the intergenic and repetitive elements are hypermethylated and silenced. In, cancer cells, these regions are found to be hypermethylated, so that these regions can now be functional. This may lead to the abberations in chromosomal structures like deletions, additions etc. of repetitive sequences or abnormal gene expression from intergenic regions.

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

METHYLATION PATTERN OF MATERNAL ALLELE:  The Imprint control Region (ICR) in maternal allele is not methylated, hence, the insulator protein CTCF can bind to it and in turn blocks the binding of enhancers to igf2, the product of which act as growth stimulators. The enhancers are directed to H19 instead.

METHYLATION PATTERN OF PATERNAL ALLELE: The ICR is methylated which might even extend to the promoter of H19. The insulator protein CTCF cannot bind to it and hence the enhancers ( that has more affinity to Igf2) binds to Igf2 and expresses it.

         Since, only one allele of Igf2 is active , limited amount of growth promoter is produced and hence growth of cell is normal.

WILM'S TUMOUR: In this tumour the maternal imprinting is disrupted as the ICR in maternal allele becomes hypermethylated, so that CTCF cannot bind to it and hence the Igf2 cannnot be insulated from the enhancers. Basically, the effect is like having two paternal alleles. The result is the over expression of Igf2 which is a growth promoter. Consequently, it leads to tumor.
