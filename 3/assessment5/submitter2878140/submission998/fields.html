Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

The C-G dinucleotides commonly named CpG islands are located near or in promoters. The Cytosine has the ability to be methylated and when this occurs at promoters, the related genes get silenced; but, in a normal cell, promoters tend to be unmethylated (expressed). In cancer cell occur abnormal methylation; thus, CpG islands at promoters results hypermethylated and the repetitive elements and introns (normally methylated) results unmethylated or hypomethylated.

In cancer, when the CpG islands in promoters of tumour suppressor genes gets methylated, this leads to silencing, and like this genes are in charge of decrease proliferation of the cell, this will end up in uncontrollable cell division. Additionally, the activation of oncogenes by hypomethylation of their promoters leads to a continued growth promotion.

The Knudson hypothesis says that multiple hits are needed to cancer occur, because if just one copy of a tumour suppressor gene or an oncogene is failing, that wouldn´t be enough to cause tumours.

By other hand, the introns must been methylated (silenced), the function of them is maintaining genomic integrity, because genomic instability would leads to point mutations, illegitimate recombination, etc. The repetitive elements have the capacity of activate or inactivate the expression of other genes; normally, they can alter the genome sequence and then change the expression of genes, that´s why they must been heavily methylated. In cancer, repetitive elements use to be missexpressed. piRNA have an important role in stem cells and when get damaged, increase the number of transposons and thus, the transcription of wrong fragments .

Some lncRNA like HOTAIR could get overexpressed in some kinds of cancer and this can retarget PRC2 (molecule which lays down H3K27 methylation) so, we obtain an embryonic kind of tissue (with no methylation or hypomethylated).

Quoting Marnie Blewitt: unlike mutations, epimutations are reversible!

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

Genomic imprinting happen when just one allele of a particular parent is getting express for a specific gene. When the Imprint-Control-Region (ICR) gets methylated, for example, in the maternal allele, it´s maternally imprinted. Here methylation doesn´t mean gene silencing. Methylation lays down during primordial germ cell, where parent-of-origin specific marks get established.

The maternal allele of the H19/Igf2 cluster is normally unmethylated at the ICR and CTCF protein binds to it. This union insulates the enhancers from Igf2, thus, the enhancers enhance the expression of H19. In the paternal allele, ICR is methylated so, CTCF can´t get bind; by consequence, the enhancers enhance Igf2. The expression of H19 doesn´t occurs because the heterochromatin at the ICR spread to H19 location.

In Wilms-tumour the cluster is maternally imprinted;both alleles are methylated at ICR, thus, CTCF can´t recognize it and then bind, so H19 gets silenced and Igf2 gets enhance, resulting in a doubled expression of Igf2 which leads to Wilms-tumour.

Disruption of Igf2 turned it into an oncogen and disrupt Kcnq1 cluster, leading to silencing a tumour-suppressor gene call cdkn1c; improving the probability of develop tumours.

Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 

Decitabine is a drug which inhibits DNMT, an enzyme which establishes methylation. DNMT1 recognize hemimethylated DNA and establish methylation on the synthesized strand. But Decitabine, an analogue of nucleoside, incorporate into the new strand, and when the enzyme DNMT1 comes, it gets bound permanently and can´t be released. When DNMT1 fails, the DNA in the daughter cell will keeps being hemimethylated and methylation will dilute out through cellular division. Cancer cells would be more affected because they replicate more often, than normal cells.

DNA methylation helps to silence the expression some genes; whenis present, the chromatin turn into a heterochromatin conformation. This heavily methylated conformation makes transcriptionally inactive the genes inside of it. However, Decitabine capacity able to take off the repression and reestablish their normal expression. So if we could make specific this action to the tumour-suppressor genes, we could restore the cell ability to fight cancer.
