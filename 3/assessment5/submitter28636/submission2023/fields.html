Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 

 Decitabine, epigenetic inhibitors, is described in the article “Cancer’s epicentre”.

Decitabine is sold as Dacogen by Eisai, a Japanese company, and is used to treat myelodysplastic syndromes, the precursors of acute myelogenous leukaemia.

Decitabine is an epigenetic inhibitor drug and belongs to the  class of DNA-demethylating agents.

Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect because DNA methylation is one of many epigenetic mechanisms that cells use to control gene expression. Tumor suppressor genes are often turned "off " in cancer cells because of hypermethylation, and Decitabine can turn them "on".

This change would be stable because epigenetic changes are inherited on during cell division to daughter and granddaughter cells  and finally all cells would have tumor suppressor genes turned "on". That is why Decitabine will stop a cancer growing without killing all its cells.
