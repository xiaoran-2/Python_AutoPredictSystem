Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

CpG islands are generally found at gene promoters. Methylation of one of these
islands blocks the promoter it is situated at, therefore silencing gene expression.
It is an important mechanism in the formation of heterochromatin. CpG islands are
usually unmethylated in actively transcribed genes. In cancer though, CpG island
methylation is often found near tumour supressor genes, blocking their expression
and aiding the aberrant growth of cancer cells. Disruption of CpG island
methylation can lead to inappropriate expression/overexpression of genes which can
lead to various negative outcomes for the subject dependent on the gene in
question. DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is a
safeguard to ensure genomic stability. Cancer disables this safeguard by removing
methylation in these places. Disruption of methylation in intergenic regions and
repetitive elements increases the risk of illicit transposition events like
deletions, insertions and translocation of gene parts.

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

The imprint control region (ICR) of the H19/Igf2 cluster is located between the
Igf2 gene (upstream of ICR) and the H19 gene (downstream of ICR). Furthermore,
downstream of H19 sits a series of enhancers. On the maternal allele, the
unmethylated ICR facilitates the binding of CTCF, which acts as an insulator that
blocks the enhancers from reaching Igf2 and leads them to target H19 instead. On
the paternal allele, CTCF cannot bind to the methylated ICR, therefore the
enhancers activate Igf2 expression, while DNA methylation spreads from the ICR to
H19. Loss of imprinting on the maternal allele leads to overexpression of Igf2,
which is an oncogene. Furthermore, the H19/Igf2 cluster is linked with the Kcnq1
cluster, which on the paternal allele suppresses the expression of Cdkn1c (a tumour
suppressor). This combination is ideal for aberrant growth, like in Wilm's tumour
for example. Loss of imprinting is often observed in cancer as a lot of imprinted
genes (like Igf2) are related to growth.

Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 

Decitabine is a DNMT (de novo methyltransferase) inhibitor. DNMTs are involved in
laying down and maintaining DNA methylation. After mitosis of a previously
methylated cell, the new cells are hemi-methylated (methylated on only one strand)
until DNMTs complete the methylation again. DNMT inhibitors bind DNMTs, so they
cannot maintain DNA methylation, how exactly this is accomplished though is not yet
clear. Subsequent cell division cycles will further decrease the methylation level,
increasing for example the (previously blocked) expression of tumour suppressor
genes therefore slowing or even halting tumourigenesis.

Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.

Epigenetic changes are mitotically heritable. That means epigenetic marks are
maintained over subsequent cell divisions. There are two major periods were
epigenetic marks are cleared and subsequently reset: During 1. early development
(pre-implantation period) and 2. primordial germ cell (PGC) development. Disruption
of these periods of epigenetic reprogramming can lead to a multitude of negative
effects for the subject. Incomplete clearing or failure to reestablish epigenetic
marks during early development can lead, for example, to imprinting disorders. In
the case of PGC development, disruption can lead to possible defects in the
subjects progeny, hamper his ability to create viable offspring or even render the
subject sterile. It therefore is not recommended to treat patients with epigenetic
drugs during onset of puberty.
