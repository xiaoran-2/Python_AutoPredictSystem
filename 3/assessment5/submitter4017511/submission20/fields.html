Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

CpG islands are normally not methylated. These islands are called promoted CpGs, and they are involved in the expression of a gene. In normal CpG islands in a normal gene, DNA methylation, if present, functions as a gene silencer by formation of heterochromatin. However, in a cancer call, DNA methylation occurs at promoter CpG islands, at an increased rate This hypermethylation of the promoter CpG islands, causes the root gene to be silenced. This hypermethylation can cause tumor-suppresor genes to be silenced, and allow cancerous tumors to grow. This disruption in CpG methylation can also cause mental retardation, such as the Fragile X syndrome, caused by the gene FMR1, as well as Rett syndrome. Cancers that involve hypermethylation of CpG islands don't involve the same islands being hypermethylated in all the different types of cancer. That is to say, the hypermethylated islands differ with each cancer type. There are also panels of biomarkers that use this CpG hypermethylation to diagnose the types of tumors, such as for glioma or neuroblastoma. These panels of biomarkers can also be used to find tumor sub types, and the origin of the primary tumor versus the metastisised ones, for more effective treatment. 

Intergenic regions and repetitive elements in a normal call are methylated, and the CpG islands are unmethylated, that is, there is no DNA methylation taking place at those islands. In a cancer cell, the CpG islands are hypermethylated, and the intergenic regions are repetitive elements are hypomethylated. This "swap" in the methylation on a genome can supress the tumor supressor genes and potentially express the oncogene. This hypomethylation can also lead to genomic instability, which can lead to the illegitimate recombination between repeats, such as translocation Deletions and insertions in a genome can also be a result, leading to cancer.

For example, in cancer, many tumor-suppresor genes are silenced by hypermethylation by those specific genes. Decitabine can cause the hypermethylation of those genes to be reversed, hypomethylating and activating them, potentially reducing the rate of tumor growth, or stopping it completely. It may also be able to activte the tumor supressor genes to the point where the tumors may actually be able to start shrinking, effectively helping cure cancer tumors, increasing the chaces of a good prognosis for patients.

Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.

DNA methylation is a mitotically hertiable process, which can last throughout the lifespan of a cell. Therefore, when DNA methylation occurs, it is copied again and again, from a cell to its daughter cells, and to their daughter cells and so on. Since cancer cells replcate more rapidly, any effect on DNA methylation in a cancer cell is passed on more quickly and can effect the tumor more severely. 

Sensitive periods, in which epigenetic markers are more vulnerable, can be during the development of germ cells in children who have cancer, as well as during the pregnancy of a woman who has cancer. The drugs being administered to the patients may adversely effect these germ cells or the fetus, which may lead to infertility, when it is given to a child, or a miscarriage in a pregnant woman.
