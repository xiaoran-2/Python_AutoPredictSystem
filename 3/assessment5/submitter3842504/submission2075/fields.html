Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

DNA methylation is a major epigenetic modification that is strongly involved in the physiological control of genome expression and can almost exclusively happen only on cytosines preceding guanine (CpG) in the DNA sequence.

In mammalian genomes there are genomic regions that contain a high frequency of CpG sites and arecalled CpG islands.  CpG islands frequently contain gene promoters or exons.

Though the majority of CpG sites in human DNA are methylated, CpG islands are typically unmethylated.  However, in cancer cells, focal hypermethylation of promoter-associated CpG islands are frequently seen.  Though most of these alterations in cancers are thought to be harmless, cells in whichthe tumor-suppressor genes are silenced may have a survival advantage over normal cells and contribute to the development of cancer.

On the other hand intergenic regions and repetitive elements are usually densely methylated in normal cells and it helps to maintain genomic stability.

However, the hypomethylation in those regions is frequently found in cancer cells and leads to genomic instability, such as illegitimate recombination between repeats, activation of repeats, or activation of cryptic promoters and disuruption to neighbouring genes.  Genetic instability causes genomic abnormality, such as translocation, deletion, and insertion, which contributes to the development of cancer.

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

About 70% of Wilms’ tumor cases were found to have an overexpression of insulin-like growth factor 2 (IGF2) and inactivation of H19 was also reported in a number of cases.

Since IGF2 is a fetal growth factor gene with an important role in cancer development and H19 is a maternally expressed non-coding RNA with possible tumor suppressor effect, over-expression of IGF2 and silencing of H19 can cause the development of cancer. The expression of the insulin-like growth factor 2 (IGF2) and H19 genes is reciprocally controlled by genomic imprinting.  The Imprinting Center 1 (IC1) of IGF2, which is a methylation-sensitive chromatin insulator, is located between IGF2 and H19.

On the paternal allele the DNA methylation of IC1 prevents the binding with the multi-zinc-finger proteinCTCF and allows the enhancer-mediated activation of IGF2, whereas the H19 promoter ishypermethylated and silenced. However, since IC1 on the maternal allele is not methylated, it binds with CTCF and this binding prevents the activation of the IGF2 promoter but activates the H19.  

The disruption of the H19/IGF2 cluster, such as uniparental paternal disomy (UPD) can cause overexpression of IGF2 and inactivation of H19 and lead to the development of Wilms’ tumor.

Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 

5-AZA -2’-deoxycytidine (decitabine) was first synthesized in 1964.  At first decitabine was known as anantileukemic agent more potent than cytosine arabinoside but in 1979 the unique capacity of this drug as an inhibitor of DNA methylation was discovered.  Though its approval for the treatment of cancer took a long time, in 2006 it was approved for the treatment of Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS).

Decitabine is activated by phosphorylation and is incorporated into DNA by DNA polymerase.  The formation of a covalent complex between decitabine and DNMT1 at CpG methylation sites results in the inactivation of DNMT1.  DNMT1 inhibition results in DNA hypomethylation.  Since a variety of tumorsuppressor genes are inactivated by promoter methylation in tumor cells, DNA hypomethylation results inthe reactivation of the silenced tumor suppressor gene and restoration of the gene function, which inducescellular differentiation, senescene and apoptosis.

Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.

Since DNA methylation is passed on during cell division to daughter cells until it is actively erased and doesn’t return once erased, if DNA methylation is altered by drugs, it may cause a life-long effect.  The demethylation drug may also reduce the level of DNMT1 which recognizes hemi-methylated DNA and causes the methylation of the non-methylated strand, and also the reduction of DNMT1 may result in a passive demethylation process during cell proliferation for a long time.

It is known that in human development there are times when environmental factors have a large impact on the body through epigenetic mechanisms and they are called sensitive periods.

From the epidemiological studies, peri-conceptional period, which is during embryogenesis and gametogenesis, and slow growth period (9-12 years in grandfathers, 8-10 years in grandmother, andfetal/infant life for grandmothers) are thought to be sensitive periods.

If the DNA methylation is altered in these sensitive periods, it may cause a variety of abnormalities in the patients, children, or grandchildren.  In light of that, we should avoid the use of epigenetic drugs during that period.
