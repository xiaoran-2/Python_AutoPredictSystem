Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

According to Knudson hypothesis cancer is the result of diverse failures in DNA. But we can find some characteristics common in all cancers.

If we have a look inside the genome we can resume the regions in three groups:

-          Genes: that contains information to produce proteins

-          Intergenic regions: as the name indicates regions between two genes

-          Repetitive elements: regions that we can find lot of times in the genome

Those regions are modulates by epigenetic factors, as methylation. Methylation  regulates the genetic expression by two ways. The first one because maintain the chromatine in the close structure and the second one because inhibit that transcription factors join with chromatine.

That’s why methylation play an important role in studies against cancer, because is one mechanism of regulation of gene expression.

In normal cells we will find intergenic regions, introns and repetitive elements (but not CpG islands) methylated, meaning these regions are inactive. It doesn’t occur the same with CpG islands (regions near tumour suppressor gene’s promoter) that are not methylated, so activated.

In cancerit happens the opposite thing; intergenic regions, introns and repetitive elements (but not CpG islands) are not methylated (heterochromatine hipomethylation that causes genomic instability), but CpG islands are methylated (hypermethylation of CpG islands) so suppressor promoter genes are inactivated.

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

It seems to be relationship between Igf2 (Insuline Growth Factor 2) a gene that play an important role in fetal development and H19, a gene that codify for a noncoding RNA.

In normal cell the gene Igf2 from father locus is active, this fact made that upstream H19 sites are methylated, making H19 parental locus inactive. This fact generates a positive response to cell growth. In the other hand, the Igf2 from mother locus is inactive so there are not methylation upstream H19 gene, what it means that maternal H19 gene is active and it will regulate cell growth (less cell growth).

In Wilm’s tumour cell, the paternal Igf2 gene is active and H19 inactive, as in normal cell, but maternal pattern is changed. Igf2 is active so H19 is inactive. This fact produces a high cell growth.
