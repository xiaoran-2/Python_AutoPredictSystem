Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

DNA methylation is important for the normal function of the cell. The alteration of DNA methylation contribute to cancer development.

DNA methylation at CpG islands silences the gene expression, being commonly presented in the promoter gene. In normal cells CpG islands are usually unmethylated. Due to that, genes are expressed, whereas in cancer cells this regions shows methylation, with the consequence of gene silencing. Typically, the genes that are silenced are tumour suppressor genes and, as their name says, they are expressed to prevent tumour proliferation.

DNA methylation of intergenic regions and repetitive elements is important to maintain the genomic integrity. That is, avoiding transpositions and illegitimate recombinations that would cause genome instability. In cancer, both, intergenic regions and repetitive elements, are hypomethylated, which causes genomic instability or activation of promoters which are generally inactive, like oncogenes ones. 

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

The loss or increase of imprinting, that means, the methylation or unmethylation of the imprint control in both alleles causes the expression or silencing of the gene from both alleles.

We will see one example, the case of the H19/Igf2 cluster, which is paternally imprinted. That means that the imprint control region is methylated in the paternal allele. Due to that, CTCF cannot bind to the insulator element and the enhancers acts on IGF2, activating it. The maternal allele is unmethylated in this region and the CTCF bind to it, so the enhancers act on H19, which silences the Igf2 expression.

In Wilm’s tumour, both alleles are methylated, which makes that both acts as the paternal ones, with the overexpression of Igf2, which is an oncogene. 

Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 

Decitabine is a DNA methyltransferase inhibitor. That means that is a drug that binds DNA methyltransferases (DNMT), interrupting the DNA methylation process. This can have an anti-tumour effect because generally tumour suppressor genes are active, but in cancer, CpG islands presents in tumour suppressor promoters are methylated, silencing them and producing tumour proliferation. With the use of Decitabine, CpG islands methylation can be inhibited with the consequent expression of the tumour suppressor gene and the anti-tumour effect.
