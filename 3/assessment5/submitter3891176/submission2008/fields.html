Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

The function of DNA methylation at CpG islands in a normal cell is silence genes but in cancer, DNA methylation at CpG islands becomes denser and it progresses over time, it means that hypermethylation occurs. The CpG islands are in the promoters of tumour supressors genes and when DNA methylation happens, it silence this supressors genes. In other words, tumour supressors genes can't be expressed. Disruption of DNA methylation appears to suppress tumorigenesis.

The function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements in a normal cell are maintain genomic stability, so in cancer, there is a genomic instability such as illegitimate recombination between repeats, activation of repeats and transposition, activation of cryptic promoters and disruption of neighbouring genes, because of intergenic regions and repetitive elements are hypomethylated. Disruption of DNA methylation appears to enhance tumorigenesis. 

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

In the maternal allele, the ICR is unmathylated and CTCF 4 bind is insulator element, thus the enhancers will act in H19. But lgf2 will be silent, so we don't see expression. In contrast, the ICR is methylated on the paternal allele so the enhancers can act in lgf2 because CTCF 4 is not binding to insulate the ICR and lgf2 can be expressed.

In Wilm's tumour, the ICR is methylated on the maternal allele, so it can expresses lgf2 such as on the paternal allele occurs. Therefore we have a double dose of lgf2 and we know that lgf2 is growth promoting, then this is what happens in this tumour. 

Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 

Decitabine belongs to DMNTi class of epigenetic inhibitors and is used to treat myelodysplastic syndromes, the precursors of acute myelogenous leukaemia. This inhibits DNA methyltransferases 1, nucleoside analogues get incorporated into the DNA, and then, when the DNA methyltransferase 1 comes along to bind that nucleotide to then copy the methylation to the daughter strand, that DNA methyltransferase is bound irreversibly and it can no longer be released. Aditionally this methyltransferase inhibitor is division dependent so you have to have the cell replicating and cancer cells which are dividing faster than other cells in the body will be more severely affected.
