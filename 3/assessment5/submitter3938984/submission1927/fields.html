One of the facts that can cause aberrstions in epigenetic control seen in cancer is DNA methylation. In normal cells, the CpG island are hypomethylated while the intergenic regions and repetitive elements are methylated.

On the other hand, cancer cells are more likely to be methylated and the intergenic regions and repetitive elements are hypomethylated. This process is called CpG island hypermethylation and it inactive a tumour suppressor gene. Imprinting genes are those genes that should be displaying mono allelic parent-of-origin specific espression, which they no longer show bur rather became expressed for both parental alleles or silent for both parental alleles. The reason for this to occur in cancer is that this gene is responsable for growth ( promoting or repressing ).

In normal cells, on the maternal allele the control region is unmethylated therefore the CTCF binds to it which inhibits the Igf2 and promotes the H19. On the paternal allele, the control region is methylated which makes the enhancers act on the Igf2 .

In cancer cells, bothe the maternal and paternal control regions are methylated which causes a double dose of Igf2 which is a growth promoting. This type of cancer is called Wilm's tumour.
