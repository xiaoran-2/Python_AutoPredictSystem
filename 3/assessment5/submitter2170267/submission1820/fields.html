Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

                        

CpG islands usually keep free of methylation independent of their activity state. However, CpG islands from cancer cells promoters tend to become hypermethylated. This hypermethylation causes the silencing of genes which expression is controlled from that promoter.

The hypermethylation of CpG islands in promoters of tumor suppressor genes is related with the apparition of tumor cells. The fact that DNA methylation, as epigenetic modification, is mitotically heritable makes possible the continuing growth of the tumor.

 

DNA methylation on intergenic regions and repetitive elements of the genome is related with the maintenance of genomic instability. The alteration of methylation in those regions, in towards to hypomethylation causes modifications on the genomic structure. This hypomethylation of repetitive elements could cause aberrant paring of non-homologous chromosomes. Moreover, the hypomethylation on transposable elements involves a failure on their silencing letting them to move through the genome.

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

                                        

The H19/Igf2 cluster is regulated by imprinting. The cluster is composed by Igf2 locus, CTCF binding site, H19 locus and enhancer’s site.

In a normal cell, the paternal copy of this cluster is methylated at H19 locus which avoid the union of CTCF with its binding site. This cause the silencing of H19 gene and the activation of Igf2. The maternal copy does not have methylation at these locus so the CTCF is able to bind on its biding site causing the transcription of H19 gene and the silencing of Igf2.

 

In Wilm's tumor, there is a change on the imprinting status of the maternal copy making it work like the paternal one. This is because the H19 locus is methylated in both copies. The consequence of these change is the over expression of Igf2 which is a growth factor involved in the apparition on tumor cells.

Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.

                       

DNA methylation is an epigenetic mark considered quite stable. Once these marks are set on the genome, they have a mitotically heritance ensuring that are maintained on the descendant cells.

There are some sensitive periods in which the environment can influence on the establishment of the DNA methylation epigenetic marks. It is known that there are two main periods in mammals which can be consider as sensitive ones. They are the early development and de primordial germ cells development. In these two periods the epigenetic reprograming takes place and there are already some scientific studies that indicates the influences of the environment (such as drugs or others molecules) in the final result of the epigenetic marks establishment.

In the case of the patients treated with a drug that alters DNA methylation patterns, the success of the treatment in combination with chemotherapy is related with the fact that the tumor cells are undifferentiated and therefore they are in a sensitive period.

It would be advisable to study the consequences that these kind of treatments may trigger on normal cells. Specially cells which are still on a sensitive period after born as the primordial germ cells.
