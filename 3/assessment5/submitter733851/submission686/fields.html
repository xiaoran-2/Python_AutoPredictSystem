Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

CpG islands are short DNA sequences different from the average genomic pattern. These sequences are CG-rich and normally nonmethylated. Most of them are sites of transcription initiation, thus silencing promoters in this region of the DNA through CpG methylation is important to gene activity regulation. Alterations in DNA methylation are common in a variety of tumors. In carcinogenesis, epigenetic disrupted mechanisms that regulate gene expression result in hypermethylation of CpG islands, in a way that tumor suppressor genes do not repond to normal stimuli. Recently global hypomethylation has also been recognized as a cause of oncogenesis. Generally the genome is methylated in the repetitive elements, intergenic regions and hypomethylated at CpG island. DNA methylation usually refers to downregulation of transcriptional processes, avoiding harmful consequences once it can prevent transcription from happening in places where it should not occur (intergenic regions and repetitive elements). Transcription still happens through intergenic regions and repetitive elements however at a much lower rate than it would otherwise when there is no disruption in the process. The nonspecific transcription is a waste of time and energy (resources in the cell are limited)
and it can be potentially harmful if disrupted transcription leads to downregulation of a gene by RNAi pathways.

 

 

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

Genomic imprinting is a process by which there is favored expression of a specific parental allele depending on its origin either materna or paternal. Loss of imprinting is a known feature of tumour cells. In such cases instead of monoallelic expression theses cells show parental alleles that are silent from both parents. One of the ways one could explain this fact is that these imprinted genes are somehow involved in promoting or supressing growth. IGF2 is methylated on the paternal allele (the enhancers act on IGF2) but it is unmethylated on the maternal allele (the enhancers act on H19). If there is a loss of imprinting it will lead to expression of Igf2 on the maternal allele as well displaying a double dose of it.This is what happens in Wilm's tumour, a condition based on this aberrant activation of the normally repressed maternal allele.

 

 

Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 

Decitabine is a DNA-demethylating agent. The antineoplastic effect of this drug is based on direct incorporation into DNA, it inhibits DNA methyltransferase which cause hypomethylation of the DNA then cellular differentiation or apoptosis. The hypomethylation state induced by the drug acts to reestablish the normal function to genes that related to the control of cellular differentiation and proliferation.
