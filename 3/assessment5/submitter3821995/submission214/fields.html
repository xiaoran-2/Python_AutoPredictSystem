The insulin-like growth factor 2 (Igf2) and H19 are reciprocally imprinted and closely linked. Igf2 transfers a fetal growth-factor and is over expressed from the maternal allele. DNA methylation is, therefore, one of the epigenetic modifications that are involved in parental imprinting. Thus, the imprinted gene performs a crucial role in placental division, growth and function, with profound effects on the development of the fetus. In humans, therefore, the H19 and IGF2 are imprinted although imprinting of IGF2 remains controversial.

In maternal allele, usually one chromosome from the mother is responsible for the expression status. Therefore, imprinted genes are expressed from only one chromosome in an apparent of origin-dependent manner.

In Wilm’s tumour, both the Igf2 and H19 adopt a paternal type methylation pattern on both parental chromosomes (Yang et al., 2013). This results in early changes in the embryonic development, leading to numerous changes in further development and growth.
