Methylation and Cancer

 Epigenetic states are not definitive, and changes occur with age in response to environmental stimuli. Epigenetic phenomena are mediated by a variety of molecular mechanisms: DNA methylation, post-translational histone modifications (PTMs), ATP-dependent chromatin remodeling complexes, polycomb/trithorax protein complexes, and noncoding RNAs.

 Between that processes the best-characterized epigenetic mechanisms are DNA methylation, that process consists of the addition of a methyl group to the fifth carbon of the cytosine pyrimidine ring. In mammals, this occurs mostly in cytosines of the CpG dinucleotides. In the human genome, CpGs are asymmetrically distributed into CpG-poor regions and dense regions called “CpG islands,” which span the 5’ end of the regulatory region of approximately half of all genes. In normal cells, the CpG islands are usually unmethylated, whereas the sporadic CpG sites in the rest of the genome are generally methylated.

 However in the case of a cancer cell, the pattern of methylation is altered such that the CpG islands are hypermethylated, leading the silencing of tumor suppressor genes as Rb(retinoblastoma), p16INK4a (an inhibitor of the cyclin-dependent kinases), BRCA1 (involven in DNA repair) between others or genes involved in cell cycle regulation as p53, triggering a failure in the correct regulation of the development of the cell, which can thus acquire abnormal proliferative properties, and subsequently developed tumor invasion to other tissues.

 Also being present in the CpG’s islands, DNA methylation is also performed in intronic and repetitive sequences as telomeres, centrómeros and microsatellites, such regions are hypermethylated in healthy cells, this in order to prevent transcription and this genomic instability, however in a tumor cells are hypomethylated these regions, thereby promoting the expression of retrotransposable elements, for example, SINES and LINES and new insertions that may have fatal consequences
