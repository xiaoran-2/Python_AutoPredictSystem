Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

  • Normally, CpG islands are hemimethylated when we consider both the strands while the intergenic regions and the repeats are methylated.
  • In cancer cells, the CpG islands are heavily methylated while the repeats,CpG poor promoters, intergenic regions are hypomethylated and perhaps there is genome-wide hypomethylation observed
  • Normally, CpG islands are present in the promoter regions of tumour suppresor genes. Hence, if the CpG islands are hypermethylated, the tumour suppressors are silenced and hence there is a strong promotion of transcription of oncogenes and hence it leads to uncontrolled and unregulated division of cells seen in cancer.
  •  The repetitive elements , in a normal cell, are involved in genetic abberant recombination and some of them are transposons which jump within the genome causing genomic instability. The intergenic regions are non-coding regions of the genome between the exons and they help regulation of expression of several genes.The repetitive elements regions are usually methylated to keep them silenced to help in genome stability.
  • In cancer cells, the intergenic and repetitive elements are hypomethylated and as a result, they lead to genomic instability due to random jumping of gene elements within the chromosome.
  • Also,due to hypomethylation , these areas are activated and hence those regions which needs to be inactive, becomes active. This leads to an overall disorder in the genome and the epigenetic regulation is lost.

Igf2 is an oncogene which is regulated by H19, an imprint control region of Igf2. Igf2 is paternally imprinted and has H19 methylated in the paternal chromosome,so that the enhancers cannot activate the Igf2 and hence Igf2 is silenced. In Wilm's syndrome,one of the methyl transferases that methylate the H19 ICP is defective and hence the enhancers activate the Igf2, leading to tumour formation

How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment?   • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.
  • If the DNA methylation pattern is altered, as epigenetic modifications are mitotically inheritable, these changes can be carried onto the next generation. Hence a drug which has an epigenetic mode of action, can be mitotically heritable to the next progeny of cells and this is especially advantageous in cancer as the cells are continuously multiplying.
  • Sensitive period is a period during which there is epigenetic reprogramming taking place in the genome( ie. the early embryonic development and the primordial germ cell development). If there is mistake in the reprogramming , it could lead to serious genetic consequences and abnormal phenotypes.
  • When we treat patients during sensitive periods, there are possibilities that the modification we have made, to be erased during epigenetic reprogramming and our treatment becomes futile.Hence it is not advisable
