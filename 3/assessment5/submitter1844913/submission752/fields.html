Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

In the epigenetically typical mammalian cell, DNA methylation occurs infrequently at CpG islands and when it does, it generally functions to silence the expression of promoter genes. Conversely in a typical /normal cell, the intergenic regions and repetitive elements typically contain considerable DNA methylation. This packs these regions more tightly (heterochromatin) and silences transcription of these regions. Professor Timothy Bestor has proposed a “Genome Defense Theory”, asserting that DNA methylation of repetitive elements and intergenic regions protects the integrity of the genome (replication fidelity and accuracy) by preventing transpositions, duplications, and recombination errors.

In contrast, in a tumor (cancer) cell, the intergenic regions and repetitive elements have lost DNA methylation while the CpG islands have excessive DNA methylation. This hyper-methylation can spread beyond the CpG islands into near shores, silencing gene expression in surrounding areas. If the CpG island is adjacent to a promoter for a gene or cluster of genes involved in tumor-suppression, the cell loses this suppression capacity.

The hypo-methylation of the intergenic regions and repetitive elements increases genomic instability by increasing the possibility of mutations in the genome via replication errors. And the changes in the DNA methylation patterns in the genome also cause chromatin remodeling. The cancer cell nucleus structure no longer sequesters heterochromatin around the perimeter with the euchromatin in the center. Disorganization increases along with genomic instability.

Mutations (“hits”) are increasing: mutations that favor survival and replication of the cancer cell  (by the weight of natural selection) lead to tumor growth or spreading cancer.

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

A fertilized zygote normally contains 23 paired chromosomes—with each pair containing a maternal version and a paternal version. During fetal development, epigenetic marks may be laid down differently on each allele in a process called “parent-of-origin imprinting”  Some times this imprinting process can go awry with serious consequences.

One example involves the H19/ IGF2 cluster located on chromosome 11. Gene H19 codes for a long noncoding RNA that when expressed works with CTCF to bind/block the enhancer of IGF2 gene expression. IGF2 stands for Insulin-like Growth Factor 2 and is considered a major fetal growth factor.

 In normal imprinting, the maternal allele of H19 is unmethylated and gene expression occurs, leading to silencing of IGF2 expression. The paternal allele of H19 is methylated and silenced, CTCF binding does not occur, the enhancer of IGF2 is active, and IGF2 gene expression is enhanced.

In Beckwith-Wiedeman Syndrome, both maternal and paternal alleles are methylated and IGF2 is overexpressed resulting in “overgrowth” syndrome. Part of BWS is an increased risk for children to develop kidney tumors, also known as Wilm's tumor—likely because of the abnormal cell growth.

Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 

Decitabine  belongs to a class of epigenetic inhibitors that interfere with the expression of the DNMT1 gene. Since DNMT1is involved in DNA methylation, Decitabine interferes with DNA methylation, increasing hypo (un) methylated DNA. In general terms, because abnormal hypermethylation of DNA is a noted feature of cancer cells leading to silencing of tumor-suppressor gene expression, blocking this abnormal hypermethylation allows for re-activation of silenced genes.

More specifically in the article “Azacitidine and decitabine have different mechanisms of action in non-small cell lung cancer cell lines” published in the journal Lung Cancer: Targets and Therapy September 9, 2010, authors Aaron Nguyen et al propose that Decitabine increases expression of genes linked with cell differentiation (“upregulation”) and decreases expression of genes linked to the cell cycle affecting the G2/M checkpoint (“downregulation”). Hypothetically, it reverses cancer cell pluripotent- like aspect and cell skips DNA damage repair prior to mitosis.

Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.

If the DNA methylation of a tumor cell is altered during drug treatment, when the tumor cell replicates and divides, the altered DNA methylation pattern could be mitotically inherited as a metastable epiallele. This effect would persist beyond initial limited drug intervention.

A “sensitive period” in epigenetic terminology refers to a period when epigenetic alternations can affect  somatic cell gene expression in the parent generation (developing embryo) and primordial germ cell gene expression in offspring (succeeding generations).

In humans, these sensitive periods of development would be early gestation as zygotes differentiate into somatic cells. And again as soma cells are cleared of epigenetic imprinting to be reset as primordial germ cells. These PGC are, in turn, parent-of-origin imprinted through epigenetic reprogramming. Also the period just prior to sexual maturation is also a sensitive period for epigenetic alterations to have inheritable effects on the immature gametes.

Since drugs that alter DNA methylation do not specifically target only cancer cells, a drug that interferes with DNMT1 expression could have serious side-effects potentially affecting viability of developing embyros by impacting cell differentiation and leading to arrested development.

This has been the case with DNMT1 knockdown mice.
