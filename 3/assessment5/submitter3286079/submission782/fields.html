Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

In normal cells, DNA methylation at CpG islands is generally silencing promoters of genes to inhibit their expression and CpG islands of tumor repressor genes are always hypomethylated. However, in cancer cells, DNA hypermethylation is always found at CpG islands of tumor repressor genes which controlling cell growth, DNA repair and so on. This makes these genes malfunction, for example, cell cycles lose control, DNA damage can not be repaired and cells can not go apoptosis, thus,cancer cells can grow without control. Also,DNA methylation is always found at intergenic regions and repetitive elements in normal cells, these areas contain transposons, satellite repeats and transcription regulatory regions which control gene expression. DNA methylation of these regions helps keep the genome stable and regulate specific genes. But, in cancer cells, DNA hypomethylation is always found at intergenic regions and repetitive elements, which makes the genome unstable and gene deregulation. For example, disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions, which are often function as regulatory areas for the expression of genes flanking them, can deregulate the expression of certain genes which may control important cellular process,such as cell proliferation,apoptosis and so on. DNA hypomethylation of repetitive elements can make the genome unstable which will cause a high frequency of mutations within the genome and these mutations can easily cause certain diseases.

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

Genomic imprinting is that certain genes express in a parent-of-origin-specific manner. These genes often regulate cell growth and are critical for development. Thus, disruption of imprinting sometimes causes cancer. For example, in normal cells, H19/Igf2 cluster is imprinting.The imprint control region on the paternal allele is methylated, so CTCF, which is an insulator protein, can not bind to this region. This causes downstream enhancers can activate lgf2 for its expression. On the contrary, H19 promoter is silent because of the spreading of DNA methylation. For the maternal allele, imprint control region of H19/lgf2 is not methylated. CTCF can bind to this region and insulates downstream enhancers to prevent enhancers from accessing lgf2,so lgf2 can not be actived and expressed. CTCF binding also active H19 promoter. However, in Wilm's tumour, imprint control regions of the H19/lgf2 cluster is hypermethylated, this makes ICR on both maternal and paternal allleles are methylated. lgf2 on the maternal allele can also be active and expressing, so that Igf2, which is a growth factor, overexpresses in Wilm's tumour. Excessive Igf2 will promote cells proliferation without limitation, which may cause tumour formation.
