Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

Methylation at CpG islands is usually synonymous with silencing of the gene. A normal cell would display acetylation at the CpG island which allows espression of the gene and methylation of the intergenic region and repetitive elements. In cancer the CpG island displays hypermethylation which silences the gene. In many cancers genes that normally act to suppress tumouregenesis are silenced and genes responsible for cell proliferation which in the adult are normally silenced, become expressible.

Methylation of gene body intergenic intervals and repetitive elements is associated with highly expressed genes and may assist in maintaining genomic stability allowing uninterrupted expression of the gene by repressing "transcriptional noise" and inhibiting possible "antisnse transcription".

Disruption of DNA methylation of CpG islands, intergenic intervals and repetitive elements are all contributary factors in genomic wide instability. CpG island methylation is highly mitotically heritable, therefore unless the disruption is reversed, genomic instability will spread. Instability of intergenic intervals and repetitive elements may lead to deletions, insertions and/or translocations of genes within the chromosone. The degree of instability and the extent of the cellualar landscape affected will determine the nature and severity of disease expression.

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

In a normal cell the paternal allele is imprinted and therefore methylated at the imprinted region and H19 promoter allowing the enhancers, through chromatin looping to directly cause Igf2 expression. The maternal allele is not so methylated and therefore Igf2 is not expressed. On the maternal allele the unmethylated imprinted region is bound by CTCF which acts as an insulator blocking chromatin looping to Igf2. That looping then jumps to H19 to express the lncRNA thought to be an RNA reservoir.

In Wilm's tumour Igf2 is overexpressed. Igf2 is an oncogene and should be repressed/silenced in the adult. Its overexpression leads to tumour growth.

Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 

Decitabine belongs to the DNA methyltransferase inhibitor class of drugs and is a nucleoside analogue. They act as DNA demethylation agents specifaclly at the time of cell division. Because the genomic disruption is causing cell to divide more rapidly than they should, the action of this drug slows the progression of the cancer, in particular myelodysplastic syndrome which is synonymous with heavy CpG island hypermethylation.
