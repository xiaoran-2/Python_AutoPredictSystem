Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

In normal cells, CpG islands are generally hypomethylated.

In cancer cells CpG islands are more likely to be hypermethylated.

Hypermethylation in cancer is found at CpG islands in the promoter of tumor suppressor genes. Tumor suppressors are thus silenced by hypermethylation of their promoter.

While hypermethylation at CpG islands is a general feature of cancer, hypermethylation at particular CpG islands varies by tumor type.

According to the Knudson hypothesis, inhibition of tumor suppressors is not enough to cause cancer. Cancer is the result of multiple hits. In the case of tumor suppressors for exemple, inactivation of one or both alleles, coupled with the activation of an oncogene, is required for cancer to happen.

Moreover DNA methylation, once established, is mitotically heritable. Causing epimethylated cells to reproduce faster and to be rapidly selected. This occurs because silencing of tumor suppressors ultimately favors cell growth or disrupts cell death, giving tumor cells an advantage over time.

 

In a normal cell, repetitive elements and intergenic regions are generally methylated. In cancer cells, repetitive elements and intergenic regions are hypomethylated.

Genome-wide hypomethylation, while naturally occuring with time, leads to genomic instability. Thus contributing to cancer.

Hypomethylation at repetitive elements or intergenic regions contributes to general genomic instability by deletion, insertion and reciprocal translocation. Hypomethylated regions are often associated to euchromatin and allow illegitimate recombination between aligning or quasi-aligning repetitive regions. Both genetic identity and open chromatin are required.

Hypomethylation also causes the activation of repetitive elements. They transpose and disrupt the coding region of genes they jump into. Those repetitive elements can also disrupt neighbouring genes by activating their cryptic promoter, causing aberrant transcription like in the case of the Agouti or Axin gene.

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

The H19/Igf2 cluster is a good example of imprinting disruption that leads to cancer.

In the paternal allele of a normal cell, the imprinting control region (ICR) of the H19/Igf2 cluster is methylated.

Because of this DNA methylation, the CTCF insulator is unable to bind the ICR. This allows enhancement of Igf2 by downstream enhancers.

Moreover, DNA methylation from the ICR spreads to the promoter of H19 inhibiting its transcription.

Igf2 is expressed.

 

In the maternal allele of a normal cell, the ICR of the H19/Igf2 cluster is unmethylated.

The CTCF insulator protein is thus allowed to bind to the ICR. Consequently Igf2 is insulated from downstream enhancers.

Enhancement of the H19 is favored. H19 is expressed.

Igf2 is not expressed.

 

In the case of Wilm's tumor, the maternal allele is hypermethylated.

Both alleles behave like the paternal allele. Igf2 is overexpressed leading to a double dose of Igf2.

Igf2 (Insulin-like growth factor 2) codes for a major fetal growth factor. It's overexpression leads to the Wilm's tumor.

 

Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 

Decitabine is a DNA Methyltransferase Inhibitor (DNMTi). It hypomethylates DNA by inhibiting DNMTs (DNA Methyltransferases).

Decitabine is also a nucleoside analogue. It is similar enough to nucleotides to incorporate DNA upon replication.

Once incorporated, Decitabine acts specifically at the replication stage by binding irreversibly to DNMT1 during methylation of the duplicated strand.

Decitabine thus takes irreversible effect.

Because Decitabine needs duplication to take effect, tumor cells, which duplicate faster, are more severely and rapidly affected than healthy cells.

Even at very small dosage, Decitabine has very good anti-neoplastic effects.

 

Q4. Dr Stephen Baylin speculates in the Economist article that "epigenetic drugs altered the tumour cells in some lasting way that made them more susceptible to standard chemotherapy." How can drugs that alter DNA methylation have effects that last beyond the period of drug treatment? Discuss whether there are any periods of development when you would avoid treating patients with such drugs. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe how altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome.
  • Define what is meant by a sensitive period.
  • Identify sensitive periods of development.
  • Explain why treating patients during sensitive periods would be inadvisable.

DNA methylation is mitotically heritable.

Consequently, drugs that alter DNA methylation have long term epigenetic effects. This is because DNMT1 conserves methylation patterns during replication.

 

Changes in DNA methylation also occur during sensitive periods of development. Those are periods of active remodeling of epigenetic marks.

Sensitive periods happen two times during development: during germ cells developement and during early embryonic development.

 

Drugs that alter DNA methylation affect all cells not only cancer cells. This includes germ cells and embryonic cells.

For this reason younger patients, particularly those having developing germ cells should be advised not to take epigenetic treatments.

Pregnant women, especially during early stage of pregnancy should be advised not to take epigenetic treatments because it migh alter the epigenetic state of their embryo.

Treatments during sensitive periods could have severe consequences including trans-generational effects.

It could directly affect the epigenome of future children of young patients through germ cell epigenetic aberrations.

This could also affect the epigenome of children and grandchildren of pregnant women, by altering DNA methylation of early embryonic cells and germ cells of their future child.
