Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

DNA methylation at CpG islands normally functions to silence coresponding genes. In cancer, hypomethylation can actually lead to an overexpression of oncogenes. When these genes are overexpressed, cells tend to grow out of control and lead to tumors and cancerous tissue.

DNA methylation at intergenic regions and repetitive elements normally acts to suppress the transcription of these regions because they can be potentialy deliterious to the cell. In cancer this suppression does not occur, these elements are alowed to transcribe which leads to cancer and tumor growth.

Q2. Describe how disruption of imprinting can contribute to cancer, using the example of the H19/Igf2 cluster. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the methylation pattern of the paternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe the methylation pattern of the maternal allele and how this determines Igf2 expression status. 
  • Describe how imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster is disrupted in Wilm’s tumour. 
  • Explain how disrupting imprinting at the H19/Igf2 cluster contributes to cancer.

The hypermethylation of the H19 promoter on the paternal allele plays a vital role in allowing the expression of the paternal allele of IGF2. H19 expression governs IGF2 repression, and the overexpression of IGF2 is responsible for the overgrowth phenotype observed in the maternal inheritance of a deleted H19 gene. Mice inheriting only a deleted maternal H19 gene, however, displayed somatic overgrowth while mice inheriting only a deleted paternal IGF2 gene displayed somatic undergrowth when compared to wildtype mice.

The failure of maternal imprinting at the Igf2 locus predisposes individuals to several clinical conditions, including Williams tumor. The locus needs to be imprinted because over expression of the gene will lead to disease by disrupting other genes.

Q3. The Economist article “Cancer’s epicentre” describes several drugs that affect epigenetic processes. Explain how Decitabine may be used to treat cancer, with reference to effects on the epigenome. In your answer include the following points:

  • Identify the class of epigenetic inhibitors that Decitabine belongs to. 
  • Describe the impact of Decitabine on DNA methylation.
  • Describe how Decitabine can have an anti-tumour effect. 

Decitabine inhibits the activity of the enzyme EZH2, that becomes over active in cancerous cells. Patients treated with the drug saw a decline in the over methylation of tumor-supressing genens.

When EZH2 is mutated, it adds over methylates histones. This methylation silences genes that should be expressed; such as, tumor supressing genes. When treated with Decitabine, the over methylation is greatly reduced.
