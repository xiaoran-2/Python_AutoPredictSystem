Q1. Describe how DNA methylation is altered in cancer. In your answer include the following points:

  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation at CpG islands.
  • Describe how DNA methylation of CpG islands is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation at CpG islands contributes to cancer.
  • Describe the normal function of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements.
  • Describe how DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements is disrupted in cancer.
  • Explain how disruption of DNA methylation in intergenic regions and repetitive elements contributes to cancer.

DNA methylation of promoter regions inversely correlates with gene expression and is important for maintaining genetic stability.

CpG islands have a high CpG density and are usually kept free of methylation and represent regions of tissue specific methylation in normal tissues. However, in cancer cells CpG islands and CpG island shores, tend to become hypermethylated, which causes silencing of tumor suppressor genes in a locus specific manner. These genes have distinct functions in controlling cell cycle, apoptosis or DNA repair, hence, when silenced; the cell can become malignant in accordance with the Knudson hypothesis.

Different types of cancer have distinct hypermethylated locus specific regions. These regions serve as an epigenetic biomarker for many human diseases and can also be used to monitor tumor burden. They also can be used to diagnose cancer and the different patterns of hypermethylated phenotypes are associated with different disease prognoses and responses to therapy.

The methylated cytosines are found in 70–80% of CpG dinucleotides in somatic mammalian cells. These genomic wide regions correspond to repetitive elements, such as satellites, transposons and intergenic regions that are maintained in a silent state throughout consecutive mitosis by DNA methyltransferases and CpG poor promoters.

The hypomethylation genome-wide in cancer leads to genomic instability due to the activation of repeats that transpose into different regions of the genome and the illegitimate recombination between repeats.

The effectiveness of a given therapy relies basically on a context dependent manner. So, given the specific stage of the disease a specific treatment can be more prone to stop the proliferation of the cancer cell or otherwise it would enhance it. The same thing happens when different tumors are involved. Hence, knowing the DNA methylation state of a given disease would contribute for choosing the appropriate treatment under specific conditions.

Altering DNA methylation can have enduring effects on the epigenome given that the methylation patterns are heritable through generations from one cell to their daughters, thus changing DNA methylation with drug treatments would keep on transmitting the same alterate pattern when the tumour growths.

A sensitive period is that in wich at a specific state of the development of an individual the epigenetic marks suffer a reprogramming in order to give rise to the normal differentiation of specific tissues according to the stablished imprinting regions through the genome. One of this sensitive periods occurs at early stages of the formation of the embryo, at the Blastocist level, and during differentiation of the germinal cells. 

Treatment with drugs that alter DNA methylation status during this senstive periods would have an unpredictable consequence on the development of the fetus and the production of gametes by causing changes, not only in the imprinting regions, but also can cause changes in the methylation patterns of normally silenced genes that now become activated, such as the transposable elements that cause genomic instability or otherwise by silencing genes that need to be expressed in order to maintain a stable and healthy cell/individual.
